      <div class="chapter-content">
        <p>        

       
<p>The sun had almost gone down, and no game was caught. It wasn’t a very good day for hunting, Hayah thought. He had been laying in wait all day long, hoping to kill something, but still had no luck.</p>

<p>“Ah! Those Bororo again!” he cursed under his breath. “They must have driven the animals further away.” He had noticed their temporary settlements the previous day and instantly knew they were going to be trouble. It was the beginning of the dry season, and the open savannah grassland of the Adamawa region was a known haven for cattle grazing. The Bororo nomads usually come around once every two or three years to graze in the area, and each time they do, they upset the natural balance of the wildlife found there.</p>

<p>The dry season was not only favorable for pasturing in that part of the savannah, but it was also a very good time for game hunting, a sport that he loved very much. Hayah was a soldier in his early forties and still physically fit, with the relentless enthusiasm of an adolescent. During the brief periods of calm experienced in the conflict between the Fulani and the indigenous Musgum people, he liked to go inland into the lush grassland for solitude and game hunting. It provided him with the peace and quiet he needed to rest, reflect, and recuperate.</p>

<p>As he moved silently through the grass, he heard a sound that he mistook for a wild animal, so he cautiously crouched toward the source. However, as he approached the area, he saw a cow, and then another. Parting the tall grass behind which he was hiding, he saw more cows peacefully grazing in an open area. Annoyed, he considered going to meet the cattle rarer to express his frustration. But just as he was about to do so, he noticed a figure under a tree, separate from the animals. It was that of a typical Bororo girl. She was tall, slim, and had a light brownish complexion commonly found in the Fulbe tribe. Her face was turned away from him, focused on the cows. She wore a low-tied wrapper secured by numerous beads known as jigida. Her blouse was tiny, barely covering her upper torso, adorned with beads of different colors and little pieces of colorful cloth sewn onto it. Her hair was braided into cornrows of various sizes, accentuated with beads of different colors.</p>

<p>When she turned her face towards him, he held his breath. She had the most beautiful face he had ever seen. It was oval in shape, adorned with big eyes and a pointed nose. Her lips were tattooed black, and her eyes were lined with dark kohl. Three dots were marked on each of her cheeks, and she wore large round earrings on her ears. He estimated her age to be around fourteen or fifteen. She was so beautiful that for a moment he doubted whether she was human or jinn.</p>

<p>Suddenly, an uneasy yet familiar calm settled over the place. With the trained eye of an expert hunter, he swiftly scanned through the long, dry savannah grass gently swaying in the wind. And there it was, almost imperceptible and seamlessly blending into the brown grass – a lion stealthily making its way toward the herd of cattle. The lion moved slowly and cautiously, undetected by anyone, including the girl. Oblivious to the impending danger, she remained deeply absorbed in her surveillance duty, her face projecting a serene and tranquil demeanor from a distance. He couldn’t help but feel a pang of sympathy for her, knowing that she was about to experience terror beyond her imagination.</p>

<p>Silently and swiftly, he drew out his bow and arrow, taking aim directly at the beast. To his horror, he realized that the lion wasn’t heading toward the animals but rather toward the girl. “A man-eater!” he thought as he swiftly dipped his arrow in poison and prepared it on the bow, aiming at the beast. “How could this be? There are only a few people living in this area, except for the Bororo who must have arrived in the past few days.” However, there was no time for contemplation; he had to act swiftly, or else the girl would lose her life.</p>

<p>Suddenly, the lion sprang from its squatting position and charged toward the girl, just as he released an arrow in its direction. The cows scattered in various directions, fleeing for their lives. The girl turned around and witnessed the approaching beast, emitting a piercing scream. Everything unfolded rapidly, and eventually, the lifeless carcass of the lion lay at her feet. Emerging from the grass, he hurriedly approached her. She stood frozen, overwhelmed with fear.</p>

<p>“Are you alright?” he asked, gasping for air. However, she didn’t respond. She remained frozen in her fear, her gaze fixed upon the carcass. “It’s alright. It’s dead now,” he reassured her as she slowly lifted her gaze to meet his, fear still evident on her face. It then dawned on him that she couldn’t understand him since he was speaking Hausa, so he switched to Fulfulde language and asked her once again if she was alright, attempting to calm her down.</p>

<p>She must have suddenly regained her senses because she swiftly untied the veil from around her waist and covered her head. She turned her back shyly, away from him. “It’s okay,” he said again. The Bororo people, especially their women, were known to be quite reserved, and he didn’t want to upset her. “You shouldn’t be out here alone grazing, you know! These parts are teeming with wild animals. It would be better if you came in groups, or even better, let the men handle it.” Still, she remained silent. “This lion is a man-eater, which makes it even more dangerous to be alone out here. I haven’t seen one in a long time, you know.” He kept talking for about two more minutes, yet she didn’t reply. Just as he was about to stop talking, he noticed from the corner of his eye that she quickly glanced at him before running away. “Great!” he exclaimed. “She ran away and left her cows. Well, she’ll have to come back for them.” He then sat down under a tree, waiting for her return.</p>

<p>Later that day, a young Bororo girl, dressed in a typical Fulani outfit, walked down a path with a stylishly decorated calabash filled with milk. It was balanced firmly on her head, and she moved gracefully without disturbing its contents.</p>

<p>“Hey pullo, waddu kosam!” she heard a familiar girl’s voice calling out, and she turned towards the sound to see who it was. “Fantah! What are you doing here?” she asked, walking towards her, and placing the calabash on the ground. “Where did you leave the cows? It’s your turn to watch over them.”</p>

<p>“Oh, they are fine,” Fantah replied nonchalantly.</p>

<p>“You abandoned them again? Baba will be angry.”</p>

<p>“Don’t worry about them,” she said as she opened the calabash and took a sip of its contents. “Wow, you didn’t make any sales today!”</p>

<p>“Fantah! Stop drinking the milk. Dada will find out,” she said, snatching the ladle from her and using the woven raffia to cover the calabash once more. “Where did you leave the cows? What if something happens to them?”</p>

<p>“Come on, Diddi, you know those cows are smarter than most of the boys in this village… Believe me, they are.”</p>

<p>“What is wrong, Fantah?” Diddi asked, sensing that her sister was concealing something.</p>

<p>“I met a man.”</p>

<p>“You did?” Diddi gasped, and they got up and began walking towards home.</p>

<p>“Yes. Oh, he was amazing.”</p>

<p>“I thought you said all boys were foolish?”</p>

<p>“Oh, yes. But he is not a boy. He is a man,” Fantah replied, her expression filled with a dreamy gaze.</p>

<p>“Oh, wow!” her sister exclaimed in surprise, and Fantah proceeded to tell her about the handsome stranger who had saved her life.</p>

<p><em>[End of Chapter One]</em></p>

        <p>The evening sun cast its long shadow on the village of Ndokula, a quiet settlement of semi-nomadic Bororo with fewer than five hundred inhabitants. It was situated 150km west of Maroua in the lush Savannah grassland of Fombina.</p>

  <p>The Bororo formed a tightly-knit clan, often interconnected through blood and marriage. While they occasionally engaged in hunting, their primary occupation revolved around cattle herding. Every three years, they would migrate in search of greener pastures to sustain and prosper their livestock. When they moved, they would bring everything with them and establish a new settlement. Rarely did they spend more than three years in one place, and they seldom returned to previous locations. Their origins were believed to trace back to North Africa, gradually moving westward until they settled in the land of Fombina.</p>

  <p>A typical Bororo village consists of groups of thatched huts made of grass, sticks, and mud, shaped like domes. Each Ruga forms a cluster of domes surrounded by a fence constructed with beautifully woven branches, providing privacy and protection from wild animals. Usually, there is a large dome for the head of the household, a semi-large one for his wife and daughters, and perhaps another for the boys in the family. Additionally, a small round dome made of mud, called rumbu, is used for grain storage.</p>

  <p>The family structure is a father, mother, and children. Although polygamy is occasionally practiced, it is rare except in special circumstances. Marriage between cousins is common among them as it is believed to strengthen family ties and preserve wealth within the family. Intermarriage with other tribes is uncommon, resulting in a distinct similarity among the Bororo due to a shared genetic heritage.</p>

  <p>Their governance system is relatively loose. While they have a chief known as the Ardo, who is selected by the village elders, decisions on village matters are typically made by the elders themselves. The Ardo primarily holds a ceremonial position as the village chief.</p>

  <p>Malam Buba stood in front of his Ruga just before sunset, waiting for his wife to bring supper. Suddenly, his cows emerged out of nowhere and started heading toward their barn without anyone directing them. To his surprise, his daughter Fantah was not among them. As he wondered where she could be, a middle-aged man who seemed to be herding the cows approached him and greeted him.</p>

  <p>“Are these your cows?” the man asked.<br>
  “They appear to be my cows,” Malam Buba responded.<br>
  “They led me here; can you believe it? Intelligent animals!”<br>
  “You don’t say.”<br>
  “Well… the girl who was with them left, so I brought them back,” the man explained.</p>

  <p>Malam Buba’s anger flared up as he realized the situation. “You mean my daughter left my cows unattended?” he retorted angrily.<br>
  “Yes… no,” the man stammered, clearly frightened. “There was a lion. These parts are dangerous, with wild beasts everywhere. You shouldn’t allow your daughter to wander off alone.”</p>

  <p>Malam Buba gave the man a quizzical look before shouting, “Fantah!”<br>
  “Na’am, Baba,” a female voice answered from inside the compound. She emerged and stood at the doorway, surprised to see Hayah – it was the same girl who had left him with the cows. She knelt beside her father.<br>
  “Did you leave my cows in the forest by themselves and wander off?” Malam Buba questioned, but she remained silent.<br>
  “Go inside. I will deal with you later,” he ordered.<br>
  “It’s not entirely her fault. She was frightened… there was a lion…” Hayah attempted to defend her.<br>
  “A lion?” Malam Buba feigned surprise, as if he hadn’t heard him the first time.<br>
  “Yes, a man-eater.”<br>
  “That is serious.”<br>
  “Yes, but… you know, sir, you shouldn’t allow young girls to venture into the forest alone to tend to cows. It’s highly dangerous out there, teeming with wild animals.”<br>
  “Don’t worry about that. We have hunters patrolling the area, and they will take care of the beasts. Tending to cows is a task for children and girls.”</p>

  <p>At that moment, another girl, younger but equally beautiful, emerged, carrying several small calabashes filled with food stacked on top of each other. She knelt down, greeted them, and began arranging the calabashes on a mat.<br>
  “Okay,” Hayah said, realizing there was no point in trying to convince him further. “Now that your cows are safe, I will take my leave.”<br>
  “No, my friend, food is ready. Please stay and eat with me,” Malam Buba offered.<br>
  “Oh no, I must go. But thank you for the offer.”<br>
  “No, my good sir. In our culture, it is considered unfriendly for a visitor to leave a man’s house at sunset without sharing a meal with him first.”<br>
  “Okay,” Hayah agreed, not wanting to offend him. “I will stay for supper.”<br>
  “Thank you.”</p>

  <p>As they ate nyiri marori be hako mbokko from the same bowl, Hayah once again emphasized the dangers of wild animals in the area. There was also chobbal by the side.<br>
  “Rearing cows is for children and girls, young man. Our men are hunters and warriors. Believe me, we can take care of our women. No wild animal would dare harm any of them,” Malam Buba asserted.<br>
  “Very well, then,” Hayah replied, realizing it was futile to try and convince him otherwise. He decided not to mention the earlier incident, fearing it might upset Malam Buba.</p>

  <p>While they were still eating, Buba called for more water. To Haya’s surprise, it was Fantah who brought it to them. As she knelt to offer them the water, she stole a quick glance at Hayah, then shyly averted her eyes and smiled. Malam Buba noticed the exchange and said something to her in Fulfulde, causing her to cringe. She then abruptly got up and hurried into the house. Fantah didn’t appear to be very happy.</p>

  <p>“So, my stranger, what brings you to these parts? You don’t seem to be from around here,” Malam Buba inquired.<br>
  “My name is Hayah ibn Sa’id ibn Abdullah Ibn Usman. I am the great-grandson of Sheil Usman the Great. I migrated from Sokoto to Fombina on my way to Mecca for pilgrimage. However, I was appointed as the Chief Imam of Balda by Lamdo Gerei along the way, and that is where I currently reside. I occasionally venture into the forest for solitude and to indulge in my passion for hunting, which I truly enjoy as a sport.”<br>
  “Hunting is not a pastime for royals,” Malam Buba commented.<br>
  “I am aware,” Hayah acknowledged.<br>
  “What am I doing?” It suddenly dawned on Malam Buba that Hayah was a highly esteemed individual. “Allah rene Barkama,” he exclaimed.<br>
  “Oh no, Malam, you need not trouble yourself. I am a guest in your house, and you have shown me great kindness. I appreciate that and will always remember it,” Hayah responded.<br>
  “Thank you.<br>
  “No, thank you, but I must take my leave now.”<br>
  “No, Allah rene, it is already dark, and Balda is almost a full day’s journey from here. Why don’t you spend the night here and depart in the light of the day?” Malam Buba suggested.<br>
  “I don’t want to inconvenience you.” Hayah insisted.<br>
  “No, you won’t. I would be honored to host you,” Malam Buba responded.</p>

  <p>After much persuasion, he finally convinced Hayah to spend the night. Buba personally showed him to his own hut and instructed his wife to prepare the best delicacies for breakfast.</p>

  <p>At first light the next day, Malam Buba slaughtered two fowls and had his wife prepare food for Hayah to take along on his journey to Balda. Hayah was outside, preparing for his departure when Fantah approached him from behind the hut.<br>
  “Good morning, stranger,” she greeted him while avoiding eye contact, squatting down.<br>
  “Good morning, Fantah,” Hayah replied.<br>
  “You know my name?” she asked.<br>
  “Yes.”<br>
  “I hope you slept well,” she said, not looking directly at him.<br>
  “Yes, I did. Your father was very kind,” Hayah replied. She remained silent.<br>
  “I will soon be on my way,” Hayah informed her.<br>
  “To where?” she asked, showing curiosity.<br>
  “Home.”<br>
  “Home?”<br>
  “Yes, home, Balda is where I live.”<br>
  “What do you do?” Fantah inquired.<br>
  “I am a soldier, and I reside in the palace,” Hayah replied. She glanced at him as if in disbelief, and he continued, “I occasionally go hunting for fun.”<br>
  “Oh,” Fantah responded.<br>
  “Well, I must be off now,” Hayah said.<br>
  “Will you come back again?” Fantah asked.<br>
  “Do you want me to come back again?” Hayah asked, but she remained silent. Instead, she abruptly stood up and rushed out of the hut through the back, heading towards her mother’s hut.</p>

  <p>Just then, Malam Buba entered the hut.<br>
  “Allah rene, everything is ready,” he informed Hayah.<br>
  “Yes, Malam Buba, and you don’t have to address me so formally,” Hayah replied, smiling.<br>
  “If you permit, I will arrange an escort for you to ensure your safety.”<br>
  “My safety is in the hands of Allah, and I am grateful He has made me a warrior. Thank you for your concern, but it is not necessary.”<br>
  “Do come visit us again.”<br>
  “In Sha Allah,” Hayah responded, using the Arabic phrase meaning “God Willing.” With that, he took his leave.</p>

  <p>Fantah, peeking from an opening in the wall of her mother’s room, watched him depart, alongside her sister.</p>

      <div class="chapter-content">
           <p>The village of Ndokula was slowly coming to life as Hayah rode out. It was a far cry from the town of Balda where he currently resided and even less developed than the city of Maroua. However, it had a peaceful atmosphere that he loved.</p>

  <p>The morning light cast a calming grey shadow over the whole village. Young girls and boys led their herds of cattle toward the bush for grazing, disturbing the dust as they moved, and making the atmosphere even greyer. Some of the younger girls, brightly dressed in red, black, and white wrappers with short tops, adorned themselves with many bangles and beads. They carried large round calabashes filled with cow milk as they walked towards the neighboring towns to sell their wares and buy what they needed.</p>

  <p>A group of young men, dressed in black jumpers, turbans, and rubber shoes, headed westwards towards the forest to hunt. Some of the older men went towards their farms. In all, everyone was busy with one thing or another, and the town was a hustling, and bubbling beehive by the time he left. Throughout the day, he rode through the thick and dense forest, passing a few other settlements on the way because he took a different route on his way back, which wasn’t strange. Being a warrior, he was very cautious. After all, it was trickery, bravery, and tactfulness that got him to the position of the General in the Lamdo Maroua’s army, and he had since proven himself worthy of that position many times over.</p>

  <p>When he arrived at Maroua a few years back, the Fulani, who were the ruling class, were at the height of their battle conflict with the original inhabitants, the Musgum, whom they referred to as the Habe, and the Musgum were winning.</p>

  <p>Being a fellow Fulani and a blue blood, the Lamdo of Maroua welcomed him warmly, recognizing him as the prince he was. Due to his warm reception, he decided to enroll in the Lamdo’s army to use his extensive military training to support the Fulani in the war against the Habe. He was favored by providence, and the Lamdo put him in a position of command. This allowed him to introduce his own tactics and military stratagem on the battlefield, which proved to be a wise move and significantly contributed to their eventual victory. He also ensured that the soldiers carried out basic training exercises every day, maintaining their combat readiness even after the battle had been won, to avoid being taken by surprise. He completely transformed their military way of thinking.</p>

  <p>The Lamdo was so impressed with him that he promoted him to the rank of General and gave him his own daughter, Talatu, as a wife, along with the small town of Balda to oversee. Because of his extensive knowledge of the Qur’an, he also served as the Chief Imam of the town. He led prayers in the town’s central Mosque and spent his free time in between battles, teaching Qur’anic knowledge to the citizens of Balda and any occasional visitors who showed interest.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>He arrived at Maroua by sunset, finding it to be a large town in contrast to the tiny village he had just left. There were more people, more houses, and more activities, making it a more organized and bustling place. The roads were filled with horses, and the streets were lined with shops and merchants. As expected, there was an abundance of assorted goods and wares available for buying and selling at every turn. Traders constantly tried to attract the attention of prospective buyers, loudly describing the benefits of their wares, which were arranged in eye-catching styles. Sometimes, it could be annoying, but he didn’t mind, as he knew they generated the lion’s share of the city’s revenue.</p>

  <p>The houses were closely-knit together and built with well-burnt red mud bricks in cone and square shapes, in contrast to the domed-shaped huts of grass and straw of the Bororo. The people of Maroua were better dressed and more vibrant than most of the indigenous tribes neighboring them. Additionally, there were diverse kinds of people and the jobs they did to survive, making it a large pre-modern city.</p>

  <p>As the evening grew darker, the traders started closing their shops, preparing to retire for the day. Everyone seemed to be in a hurry to return to their homes. As he rode through the city, he could perceive the delicious smell of cooking from different houses, making him hungry. However, he only grimaced and rode on, as he had other pressing matters to discuss with the Lamdo of Maroua. He knew that a feast would be waiting for him at the palace as well.</p>

  <p>Usually, he visited the Lamdo every day whenever he was around, and they always ate dinner together. Balda was not far from Maroua, so he made it a duty to pay homage to his Lamdo as often as possible. Coming from a royal family, he knew how important it was to be on Lamdo’s good side, especially after the disgrace he suffered back at home.</p>

  <p>Hayah, though an aristocrat, was essentially a loner by nature who liked doing things on his own terms and in his own way, which explains why he loved hunting so much. It gave him the time and freedom to be alone with his own thoughts. He was also someone who loved to plan every action and did everything according to pre-set guidelines. Being alone helped him organize his thoughts to come up with the necessary strategies to achieve his goals. Unfortunately, being a royal, he was always surrounded by people, and that really bothered him. There were always meetings, functions, and events to attend, leaving him too little private time. To make matters worse, the people expected him to follow certain rules and traditions instituted by long-gone kings and statesmen, which he considered too rigid and archaic. His opinions and decisions, no matter how good they were, were disregarded as long as they did not conform to the pre-set norms. It was a big problem for him since he was a man of strong opinions, considered by many as strong-headed and almost always at variance with many of his courtiers. In the end, he was forced by a faction in his court to abdicate the throne, and he left Sokoto in exile, feeling shamed and angry, leaving behind his wife and children without a whiff of regret.</p>

  <p>His wife was a cousin forced on him by his father, and he married her even though he never liked her or her parents. Thus, when he sneaked out of Sokoto, he left her behind. His secret intention was to return someday to reclaim his birthright, the throne, but first, he must learn patience. The how or when might be blurry, the plan was crystal clear in his mind. He needed power and resources, both of which he didn’t have now. So, integrating himself with Lamdo Gerai was a strategy to get him all that. Fortunately, the Lamdo liked him.</p>

  <p>He stopped at the royal mosque for Magrib prayers, and the Lamdo was there too. After leading the prayers, they exchanged pleasantries with the Lamdo, who was always very happy to see him. Then, they went into the palace together. Just as he expected, they were served a feast of royal delicacies. The cooks must have outdone themselves, he thought. There was rice and milk with Chobbal, cooked lamb with vegetables (miyan taushe), roast chicken with hot yaji and bean soup, and a variety of fruits like oranges, bananas, and mangos arranged neatly in a beautifully carved calabash. At the end of the meal, they enjoyed sweet dates specially imported for the Lamdo from Arabia by the Arab merchants who did business with the city of Maroua. Everything was just so perfect, he almost forgot why he was there.</p>

  <p>After dinner, the Lamdo informed him of the situation. They were able to discuss freely and unofficially because the Lamdo considered him his friend. He was not just the general of his army but also his son-in-law, privileges that qualified him, in the eyes of the Lamdo, to be taken into confidence on important matters. The Kingdom had been peaceful so far, but long silences in those parts and time could be equated to a calm before the storm.</p>

  <p>“So, Hayah, how did the hunting go? Did you kill any game?” the Lamdo asked him.</p>

  <p>“It’s was a wonderful outing for sure,” he replied.</p>

  <p>“Oh, how so?”</p>

  <p>“I saw some interesting game, but caught none, Allah Rene.” He didn’t want to tell the Lamdo about Fantah.</p>

  <p>“Oh, better luck next time,” the Lamdo said before changing the subject. “I received a report that the Musgunawa had been launching secret guerilla attacks here and there into my territory lately.”</p>

  <p>“I knew the truce was a joke! I knew the truce was a joke!” Hayah was angry.</p>

  <p>“They have joined forces with the Massa and Sumeya and have been causing a lot of damage to some of the smaller villages around. They have been attacking the caravans on their way to the Diamaré market.</p>

  <p>They are trying to block our trading route. That will seriously affect our economy.”</p>

  <p>“Of course! Not to mention serious damage to relations between us and Yola and Tcheboa. I was informed that in the last attack, they killed over twelve people and stole their merchandise and horses.</p>

  <p>“The more horses they have, the better their military will be,” Hayah said, knowing that the Fulani’s military superiority over the other tribes was mostly due to their possession of war horses. It gave them speed, cutting down fatigue, and a clear advantage over their opponents. Now it seemed like their enemies wanted to turn back the tide and even the playing field by acquiring their own horses too. They had been doing that a lot lately by sporadic but organized attacks on traders and stealing their horses. Hayah knew it could not continue; otherwise, they would be in big trouble.</p>

  <p>“We cannot afford to let them continue.”</p>

  <p>“We have to retaliate immediately.”</p>

  <p>“Yes. When do you think it would be appropriate?” the Lamdo asked.</p>

  <p>“The day after tomorrow, I will gather my men, and we will leave after dark.” One of Hayah’s biggest assets was his combat-readiness at any given time, and that was why the Lamdo loved him so much.</p>

  <p>“Ok,” Lamdo Gerai said. “But I suggest we should try and have a peace talk with the Musgum chief first before any reprisal attack. He may not be aware of what is going on. For all we know, those attacks were sporadic and revenge-driven.</p>

  <p>“Yes, Allah rene,” Hayah said. “I will instruct the messengers immediately.”</p>

  <p>“Ok, good,” the Lamdo replied and then changed the subject again.</p>

  <p>“Talatu has been worried sick about you.”</p>

  <p>“I am fine.” Hayah said.</p>

  <p>“Of course, but she couldn’t understand why her groom of less than two weeks would just take off like that.”</p>

  <p>“What are you saying, Allah rene? Do you suggest that I stay at home with her and never go out?”</p>

  <p>“No, of course not. How could I even begin to suggest that? You are a warrior who loves the outdoors. She will just have to understand.”</p>

  <p>“Allah rene, forgive me for being so direct…”</p>

  <p>“Hayah,” Lamdo Gerai cuts him off. “I am an old man. My days on this earth are few. I gave you my daughter’s hand in marriage because you are my friend and I trust you. I also believe you are capable of taking care of her even after my death. I was just speaking in my capacity as a father.”</p>

  <p>“I promise not to let you down,” Hayah reassured him.</p>

  <p>“I trust you wouldn’t.” Lamdo Gerai agreed.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>After the discussion, he left straight for Balda. It took Hayah thirty minutes of riding through the cold night to reach the town. And on arrival, he went straight to his house. On the way to his wife’s apartment, he passed different concubines who knelt to greet him. There were about six of them of different sizes and shades. They were all beautiful, and he could have asked any of them to join him in his room for the night if he wanted. It was his right as a ruler to have his way with any of the concubines that he owned, but as tempting as that was, he would rather be with his wife. When he got to her quarters, he met with her uncle, the Waziri, at the door, who had a habit of visiting her at odd hours, and Hayah was not comfortable with it. But he couldn’t say anything since he was his in-law.”</p>

  <p>“Allah sabbine, Allah renei. Allah baru nganyo ma.” The Waziri greeted him in the customary royal way.</p>

  <p>“My in-law. How are you tonight?” He returned the greetings, completely ignoring the praise that the Waziri was lavishing him with. For some reason, the man, made him very nervous, and he didn’t like it.</p>

  <p>“I am very well, Allah rene.”</p>

  <p>“You look well.”</p>

  <p>“Thank you, Allah rene. I just came to see my daughter. She was so worried.”</p>

  <p>“Worried?” Hayah feigned surprise.</p>

  <p>“She was just wondering if you were all right, that’s all; … but I assured her that she had nothing to worry about… you know she is only a child and doesn’t know the ways of life that much…”</p>

  <p>“You have done well, my in-law. Thank you.” With that, he bade him goodnight and went inside before the conversation should be prolonged further. He didn’t particularly like the man, but there was nothing he could do about it since they were related by marriage. The Lamdo’s daughter he married was raised by the Waziri, who was the Lamdo’s older brother. For some reason, the kingmakers deemed him not worthy of the throne and gave it to his younger brother. As if that was not enough, he couldn’t bear any children, despite having four wives and numerous concubines. So, the Lamdo, out of pity and for the sake of peace, gave him his favorite daughter Talatu to raise as his own, and at the height of their victory against the Musgum, the Lamdo asked him to marry her off to Hayah. He did that as a way of giving him a powerful in-law and increasing his power. In fact, the Lamdo did his best to carry him along and to increase his potential influence, but often, he came off as pathetic and creepy, at least to Hayah.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Hayah said Salaamu Alaikum softly and walked into his wife’s luxurious room, fit for a princess. Everything about the room exuded royalty. The floor was covered with the softest of Persian rugs from Arabia, a gift from her father, Lamdo Gerai. The walls were lined with murals and calligraphy of mixed tones. The ceiling was high and domed and also decorated with beautiful calligraphy. Small windows around the room were placed high up to allow light in while maintaining privacy. There were different ceramic arrangements that gave the room an even more exquisite look. A very thick but sweet-smelling fragrance filled the air. It was coming from a small clay incense burner sitting on a small table. The charcoals were glowing hot, and he could see some of the wooden perfume that was sprinkled generously on it. The aroma was intoxicating. At the far corner of the room, his wife, the princess, was sitting on the best raffia bed that money could buy. Its mattress was filled with feathers and covered by beautiful white and brown sheets imported from Egypt and surrounded by many soft feather pillows. She didn’t look happy, he surmised. But as soon as she looked up and saw him, she got up off the bed and knelt to greet him. He said Salaamu Alaikum again and walked over to the bed to sit beside her. Her head was bowed down. She was shy, so he cupped her face in his hands and lifted it up so that their eyes could meet. She smiled shyly and looked away. She was very beautiful, the most beautiful of all the Lamdo’s daughters. He caressed her face again. She had an oval face, a pointed nose, and soft luscious lips. Her long soft black hair was skillfully braided and covered with a transparent veil adorned with jewelry. He felt so lucky to have been blessed with such a beautiful wife. He didn’t love her, but he liked her and was grateful to Lamdo Gerai for giving her to him. He then lifted her up and sat her next to him on the bed.</p>

  <p>That night, as his wife slept peacefully next to him, he was wide awake, and his mind wandered off to the little village North of Maroua and the beautiful shepherd girl he met there. Somehow, he couldn’t get her off his mind. He remembered her innocent smile and the sweet sound of her voice, and all he wanted at that time was to be with her.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>The next morning, Hayah held a meeting with his lieutenants to brainstorm the best strategy to deal with the problem at hand. There were several suggestions, with some favoring a pre-emptive strike, while others suggested pursuing more negotiations and threats, as they were uncertain about the true culprits behind the attacks. After a day of deliberations, they reached a consensus to invite the Musgum chief and elders for a peace dialogue. Hayah conveyed his decision to Lamdo Gerai, who approved it.</p>

  <p>The day after that, Hayah dispatched two messengers to invite the Sarking Musgunawa to Lamdo Gerai’s palace for a peaceful dialogue. However, later that evening, one of the messengers returned, covered in blood and visibly beaten. He recounted how the other messenger was killed, but the Sarking Musgunawa speared him to deliver a message: “We will not surrender. We are ready for you.” From that moment, it was agreed that a war would take place, and they decided to go on the offensive.</p>

  <p class="chapter-end">[End of Chapter Three]</p>

            <p>In the village of Ndokula, Fantah was coming from the stream with two water pots well balanced on her head, the smaller held by the bigger pot. She walked gracefully without letting any of the pots fall. It had been two days since the ‘stranger’ left, and she missed him already. She had been thinking about him all the time and prayed that he would come again. Suddenly, as if an answer to her prayers, she saw a familiar figure walking towards her, she immediately knew who it was. Her heart began to race wildly, to the point where she thought she was going to faint.</p>

  <p>“Fantah,” Hayah said as he approached.</p>

  <p>“Stranger, you are back.” She replied, her heart pounding.</p>

  <p>“Did you miss me?” He asked.</p>

  <p>“Should I?”</p>

  <p>“I missed you,” he said, and she turned away shyly, covering her face with both hands. He quickly grabbed hold of the pots to prevent them from falling and placed them gently on the ground. “I went to the grazing fields, but you were not there. Then I saw some girls coming from the stream, and I imagined I’d find you here.”</p>

  <p>“Why did you come?” she asked without looking at him.</p>

  <p>“I forgot something.”</p>

  <p>“What?”</p>

  <p>“A flower.”</p>

  <p>“A flower?” She was confused.</p>

  <p>“Yes. I saw a beautiful flower the last time I came here. I couldn’t stop thinking about it, so I came back to water it,” he said. She felt incredibly shy and tried to run away but he caught her by her beads, so she quickly used her veil to cover her face. “Oh, wild beauty,” he continued. “Bless me with your smile. Let me revel in your aura and presence.”</p>

  <p>“I must go now. My mother will be worried.”</p>

  <p>“Stay with me some more.”</p>

  <p>“I really have to go now.” She sensed another presence in the bush and knew that they were being watched.</p>

  <p>“Okay, my love,” he said and placed the pots back on her head.</p>

  <p>“Will you come and have supper with my father?” she asked before she left.</p>

  <p>“I must go back. I have an important matter to attend to. But I promise you that as soon as I finish with it, I will be back to see you, my love,” he said, looking fondly at her. She looked shyly away again, smiling, and then started walking towards home. He watched her disappear into the village before climbing his horse and riding back to Maroua with a satisfied grin on his face.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>The next morning, Fantah went to her paternal uncle’s house on an errand for her mother. She visited them occasionally, and they loved her as a daughter.</p>

  <p>As she approached the house, she felt like she saw someone hiding in the bush. The person peeked from behind a shrub. Their eyes met, and he quickly ducked. Immediately, she knew who it was—her annoying cousin, Sa’adu. He liked her but didn’t know how to approach her, so he goes into hiding whenever he sees her. Her parents talked about betrothing them to each other, but she didn’t like the idea. It wasn’t like she didn’t like him; she only liked him as a brother and did not consider him marriage material.</p>

  <p>She reached the front of the Ruga. “Assalamu Alaikum,” She said, and a familiar male voice answered, “Alaikum Salaam. Yottu mingel’am.” She walked into the house and met her uncle sitting on a mat in the Jauleru. She knelt and greeted him.</p>

  <p>“Jambanduna Bappa?” she said.</p>

  <p>“Jam. Noi Chomri?” he answered. He asked her about her family, and she said they were fine before going into the compound to see her aunt.</p>

  <p>While they were talking, her uncle came into the compound and asked if she saw Sa’adu outside. She said no.</p>

  <p>“Maybe he is with the cows,” her uncle said.</p>

  <p>“Okay,” she replied, with her head faced down, shyly.</p>

  <p>“You know, Fantah…,” her uncle started. “You have grown so big. I hope Sa’adu will be able to brave the Sharo this year.”</p>

  <p>Both he and his wife laughed. Fantah smiled while still looking down.</p>

  <p>“I am sure he will,” her aunt said. “He has even been practicing.”</p>

  <p>“Has he now? I hope he won’t disappoint.” They both laughed again.</p>

  <p>“Mi hoti Bappa.” She said and got up to leave.</p>

  <p>“Greet your father and tell him that I will come and see him later,” her uncle said.</p>

  <p>“Toh Bappa,” she replied before leaving.</p>

  <p>She wasn’t happy as she thought about it on her way home. She really didn’t want to marry her cousin. She was in love with that 'stranger’ who came into her life a few days back, and he was the only one she wanted. She said a silent prayer, hoping he would come and rescue her from the situation.</p>

  <p>“Derdirawo, mihomnima,” she heard a familiar voice say from the bushes.</p>

  <p>“Sa’adu, Vurtu!” she said annoyed. He came out but couldn’t look her in the eyes.</p>

  <p>“I just wanted to say hello. I saw you going to our house. How are your parents and Diddi?”</p>

  <p>“They are fine, and when was the last time you visited them?” It was a rhetorical question.</p>

  <p>“I saw you talking to a stranger yesterday. Who was he?”</p>

  <p>“It’s none of your business,” she said and continued walking. She knew he was the one watching them from the bushes.</p>

  <p>“Your hair looks nice, and the Lalle on your hands are nice too,” he said, following her.</p>

  <p>“Don’t you have cows to tend to? Leave me alone!”</p>

  <p>“Okay. I will see you then.”</p>

  <p>“Bye,” she said and hurried towards home.</p>

  <p>As she approached the fields, she saw her sister Diddi with their neighbor’s son, Murtala. She was letting him drink from the milk she was supposed to sell. The boy was sixteen, while Diddi was thirteen. Fantah hid in the bushes and waited until the boy left before coming out to confront her sister.</p>

  <p>“So, how much did he pay, hmm?”</p>

  <p>“Fantah, where did you come from?” Diddi was surprised.</p>

  <p>“This is what you do, ko? You come here and give Murtala free milk, and then when I ask you for some, you say Dada will know.”</p>

  <p>“Please don’t tell Dada. I only gave him a little.”</p>

  <p>“Do you like him?” Diddi looked girlishly away. “Wow! I hope he can brave the Sharo for you.” She went to sit near Diddi. “I know what it is like. I like my ‘stranger’ too, you know, your heart beating fast every time you see him. The heat you feel on your face every time you think of him. It is the best feeling ever. I wish my stranger is here too. I would love to see him again.</p>

  <p>“Do you think Baba will let me marry him?” Diddi asked.</p>

  <p>“Well, if he can brave the Sharo, I am sure Baba will have no objections.”</p>

  <p>“I don’t want him to go through that. He will be hurt.”</p>

  <p>“Don’t worry. He will be fine. Now, let me have some milk.”</p>

  <p>“Fantah, Dada will know,” Diddi tried to drag the calabash of milk away from her.</p>

  <p>“Oh, it’s like that, ko? Fine, I will tell!”</p>

  <p>“Okay, just a little.”</p>

  <h2>Chapter Five</h2>

  <p>The war against the Musgum took place in the valley at the foot of the Mandara mountains where the Habe were supposed to be hiding. Their forefathers were driven there by the Fulani in the early 17th century, during the Jihad led by Modibbo Adama, a disciple of Usman Dan Fodio. They hid in the rocky terrain because the Fulani cavalry could not follow them into the crevices. The Musgum, along with the Massa and Sumeya clans were the original inhabitants of the region but the Fulani were settlers-nomadic cattle herders who wandered into their territory. The original inhabitants accommodated them, gave them lands, and they thrived. Before long, the Fulani began to take advantage of them and started claiming the land as theirs. Inspired by the conquest in Sokoto, they acquired a newfound superiority complex over the natives, whom they referred to collectively as Habe due to their practice of idol worship, while referring to themselves as Fulbe. They were not satisfied with just co-existing together, they wanted to rule over them and make them their slaves. The Fulbe bred horses and were excellent blacksmiths. Because of that, they have some of the best weapons of that time. Over the centuries, backed by Sokoto, they fought the indigenes and took over their lands and property. Those who embraced Islam were speared. Those who refused were killed or enslaved. Their women were forced to become concubines for the royal families. The lucky ones escaped and hid in the Mandara mountains. Their ancestors may have chosen to flee instead of fighting, but the new generation of the Musgum, Sumeya, and Massa were fearless. They were filled with resentment for the Fulani and wanted to take back what they believed was theirs. They had been strategizing and training for years for those self-same reasons. They came out in full force and faced the enemy with unflinching courage. The Musgum came to war with an army of one hundred and fifty men led by their chief. The army was stationed facing the East and behind them, were mountains which they intend to retreat to. Unlike the Fulani soldiers, the Musgum were foot soldiers, with only the Sarking Musgunawa and ten others on horses – the ones they stole when they attacked the traders, Hayah thought. They also had inferior weaponry when compared to the Fulani, but he also observed they were better prepared this time than in the previous battles. The Fulani on the other hand came out with an army of one thousand, three hundred and fifty men. A very clear advantage over them, but for some reason, the Musgunawa didn’t seem scared at all. Each Fulani soldier was armed with the finest sword and fortified battle regalia, most of which were bought from Arab traders. One thousand of the men were on horses while the remaining three hundred were on foot. They would be able to annihilate the Musgum army within minutes, or so they thought.</p>

  <p>For some reason, the Musgunawa did not seem afraid at all. In fact, they seem to be possessed by some strange courage. Hayah reviewed the situation from their chief to his men and couldn’t help but wonder if they had something up their sleeves or were simply suicidal maniacs. He had a gut feeling that something was amiss, but he dismissed it as mere paranoia on his part. He wasn’t in the mood to shed any blood today. In fact, since he met Fantah, he had been experiencing strange, funny changes on the inside. All he wanted was to be with her. Suddenly, he was tired of all those unending and pointless battles.</p>

  <p>Wanting to move things along, he stepped forward, flanked by his four lieutenants – Adamu, Arabu, Umaru, and Bawuro, to have a final peace talk with the Sarking Musgunawa, as it was customary before battles then.</p>

  <p>“If you surrender now, we will let you go back home alive and in peace,” Hayah Yelled.</p>

  <p>“We will not surrender. We came here to fight and avenge our forefathers,” the Sarking Musgunawa yelled back.</p>

  <p>“So be it!” Hayah yelled back and gave the signal for his men to attack. He wanted to get it over with as soon as possible and go home. In fact, based on the enemy’s fewer numbers, he surmised it would be over in a few minutes, and he would return to Maroua with a favorable battle report for the Lamdo by sundown. After that, he would leave early for Ndokula the next day to go and see his beloved Fantah. That thought filled him with so much joy that he almost forgot he was on the battleground.</p>

  <p>Within minutes, the battleground was alive with the sound of swords clanking against each other. There were screams of courage and pain. Blood was everywhere. After about ten minutes of intense battle, the Musgum army turned around and fled. As usual, the Fulani cavalry gave chase.</p>

  <p>They pursued them to the foot of the Mandara mountains, and then deep into the elevated and rocky unfavorable terrains. Just as they were about to retreat, there was a blood-curdling scream as another column of one hundred soldiers descended on them from the left and another from the right, led by Sarking Massawa, and started attacking them from both sides. The Fulani were disoriented for a few seconds due to the element of surprise of the ambush, but quickly, they regrouped and began to fight back. Their horses were having difficulties moving in formation due to the elevation of the area and the sharp jagged rocks. Because of that, the Fulani soldiers kept falling off their horses, leading to their eventual deaths, with most of their horses injured or killed by the sharp jagged rocks in the process.</p>

  <p>In the meantime, the Musgum army suddenly turned around and started to attack them from the front with renewed vigor. So, this was the trick, Hayah thought. That was why they were not scared at all. They had studied the Fulani army and had anticipated their moves. They lured them to this impossible terrain to ambush them, and it paid off. He felt raging anger at himself for this temporary setback and for his inability to have foreseen this old war trick coming.</p>

  <p>Hayah yelled orders to his men to fall back, but before they could, another column of one hundred and eighty soldiers led by the Sarking Sumeya emerged from behind the rocks and blocked them from behind. The Fulani were being hit from the front, the back, and on both sides. They were completely boxed in, and they had no choice but to fight to the death.</p>

  <p>It had been the Sarking Musgunawa’s plan all along. And it worked. He reasoned that the Fulani’s victories were not only attributable to the superior weapons and numbers but also due to their strategy of divide and rule, which had been so successful because the indigenous tribes had been fighting them separately, and as a result, facing their inevitable destruction. Not only were the Fulani in possession of superior artillery, but they also had extensive training and were always battle ready. They never laid down their arms. Therefore, they always won with minimal losses. He decided that if they were ever going to stand any chance against the Fulbe, they had to unite their forces. They also needed the element of surprise because no matter how hard they trained; they were still at a disadvantage. But with the surprise element, they might be able to confuse them just enough to deal them a blow so heavy that they wouldn’t dare come after them again. And that was exactly what happened, resulting in a very satisfying outcome indeed.</p>

  <p>But he made an error of judgment when he overlooked something very important. For the Fulbe, it was not just a war of attribution, but a war of ideology and identity. Every one of those Fulani soldiers believed that they were fighting for a higher purpose and that there would be a reward if they died in the process. Either way, they never lose. That belief filled them with a lot of courage. So, instead of cowering and succumbing to defeat, Hayah yelled at his men to fight to the death, and they were filled with renewed vigor! His men followed suit. The war began to take a different coloration. The Fulbe charges at the Habe! The Habe shouted “Eeiihoo!” and charged back at them. Every time the Habe hit the Fulbe, they hit back harder. They fought back with the ferocity of a cornered animal. The sound of metal against metal was deafening and terrifying. Heads were flying, and men were falling like leaves. It was an orgy of blood and guts. The ash-grey color of the rocks turned red with the blood of the wounded and the dead. After about twenty minutes of pure gore, the Habe turned around and fled for real this time. They were flummoxed by the turn of events against them, and the Fulbe decided it was unwise to give chase, so they tended to their wounded.</p>

  <p>The Musgum lost eighty-seven men that day, the Sumeya lost seventy-one, and the Massa lost one hundred and one. But the Fulani, even though victorious, lost over five hundred men. The element of surprise worked, and they suffered heavy losses. They may have won the battle, but it was a pyrrhic victory.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Hayah could not believe what had just happened. After ‘picking his bottom jaw off the floor’, he began to process the whole series of events in his head. They were tricked. The Habe outsmarted him. Despite his training and sharp instincts, they were still able to pull a fast one on him, and he lost almost half of his men in the process. His pride was greatly wounded, and he was royally pissed!</p>

  <p>He looked across the battlefield and saw scores of dead and wounded men and horses and felt bile climbing up his throat. It was his fault, and he knew it. Not only was he distracted by his romantic thoughts, but also ignored the warnings his gut gave him. One of his strengths was his sharp instincts which never failed him, and he was angry at himself for ignoring it. Most importantly, he evaluated the enemy by their size, which was a wrong premise in war strategy, exclusive of other factors.</p>

  <p>“We seriously underestimated them,” Hayah thought about the very important rule of war and promised never to make the same mistake ever again. He should have seen it coming. The Sarking Musgunawa was way too confident and eager for this confrontation to take place, which showed he was well prepared for it. If the trend continues, they will end up losing the war to the Habe entirely. He felt chills up his spine at that thought.</p>

  <p>His men were also angry and were going around to check for any surviving Habe, and instead of taking them as hostages, they stabbed them to death out of anger. He was too upset to stop them. It was a horrible day and he contemplated on what to tell Lamdo Gerai. Hayah was particularly upset. Not only for the losses, but because war was an area of special interest for him, and he was good at it. Coming from Sokoto, the seat of Fulani power, the place where the most famous Jihad in African history started, where every noble male was trained all their lives on warfare for the protection of their land and religion, he arrived in Maroua at the time of war with the Musgum, Massa, and Sumeya Habe, and he was instrumental in their often routine defeat by the Fulani, which endeared him to the good graces of the Lamdo of Maroua as his favorite general. The constant string of victories he had, won him favors, not just from the Lamdo but also the Love of the people. The people had heard for a long time of a great man, a savior that would come from the East to emancipate them from the Habe. For some time, he was able to fulfill that role, and they believed he was the one. But these Habe was threatening to take that away from him. He had made the grave mistake of underestimating them, but he won't repeat that again.</p>

          <li>Rwanda</li>
          <li>Tanzania</li>
          <li>Namibia</li>
          <li>Togoland</li>
        </ul>
      </li>
      
      <li><strong>Belgium's</strong> territory included:

  <p>The Berlin Conference's decisions led to the arbitrary division of Africa by the European powers, disregarding the diverse ethnic, cultural, and historical backgrounds of the African nations. The scramble for Africa had profound and lasting impacts on the continent's history, leading to colonization, exploitation, and the disruption of traditional societies.</p>

  <p>At the end of the allotment, Fombina fell into the hands of two very powerful nations: Germany and Great Britain, an outcome that spelled doom for the once-great kingdom.</p>

  <div id="page-7" class="page-container">

      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 7: A Promise Under the Moon</h1>
        <p class="subtitle">Love blossoms under moonlight, but shadows of jealousy stir nearby.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
       
  <p>As nightfall descended upon the village of <em>Ndokula</em>, Fantah and Diddi were helping their mother clean up after supper. It had been over two months since the stranger, and though he had promised to return, she hadn’t heard from him. Worry had begun to creep into her mind, fearing he might have lost interest in her.</p>

  <p>“Maybe he found someone else, someone prettier perhaps,” she said to her sister.</p>

  <p>“Kayya! That’s not possible. Fantah, there is no one prettier than you,” Diddi tried to reassure her.</p>

  <p>“Then why hasn’t he come? He didn’t even send a message.”</p>

  <p>“He said he had something important to attend to, remember? Maybe it didn’t go as planned. Perhaps he is trying to set things right before coming back,” Diddi comforted.</p>

  <p>Fantah sighed, hoping Diddi was right. She missed the stranger’s company, his mysterious aura, and the way his eyes sparkled when he talked to her. Yet a part of her felt anxious that he might never return.</p>

  <p>“Right. Maybe.” Fantah paused for a few seconds. “Or maybe he has a wife, and she stopped him.”</p>

  <p>“Useni, Fantah. Stop worrying.”</p>

  <p>“I can’t help it.”</p>

  <p>Just then, a small boy came in and told her that she had a visitor waiting in the fields. Immediately she knew who it was.</p>

  <p>“Go on, Fantah. I will cover for you,” Diddi told her.</p>

  <p>“Thanks. I will be back soon.”</p>

  <p>It was a brisk night, a mild, chilly breeze blowing across the village, quiet in the open except for the occasional chirping of crickets. The harmattan was still in full force, occasionally stirring dust in the air. She sat beside him under the tree, her head tilted down, too shy to look him in the eye. The moon was almost full, casting a bright light over them. He admired her from the depths of his weary soul.</p>

  <p>“You look very beautiful,” he said, and she covered her face with her hands. “I missed you. Did you miss me?” She did not reply. “I am sorry I took so long to come back. Things have been crazy back home, and I couldn’t just leave.”</p>

  <p>He brought gifts: a pair of gold earrings for her, and sweet dates for Diddi. She accepted them gracefully and thanked him. They talked for a while before he told her about his wife and his six concubines. She was disappointed. She wasn’t comfortable with polygamy. Her people were monogamous by nature, and polygamy was only practiced by royalty. Her family was not. She felt a tinge of jealousy. She never imagined having to share him with anybody, let alone a princess.</p>

  <p>He further explained how he came to Maroua and the events that led to his marriage with the Lamdo’s daughter. In the end, he convinced her. She realized the love she had for him was greater than any jealousy she felt for her rivals, and she smiled warily.</p>

  <p>Finally, he persuaded her to accept his proposal for marriage, promising to see her father soon.</p>

  <p>“I want you to be my wife, Fantah,” Hayah said. She smiled and looked away.</p>

  <p>“Come with me to Balda.”</p>

  <p>“Yes, I will,” she said diffidently, then got up and ran back toward the house. Just as she approached, she met Sa’adu, who wasn’t looking happy.</p>

  <p>“Where are you coming from?” he asked.</p>

  <p>“And how does that concern you?” she snapped.</p>

  <p>“It is my business. You are my future wife. Our parents promised you to me when you were just a baby.”</p>

  <p>“That is just it, isn’t it? They never asked me if I wanted to marry you. Well, I won’t!”</p>

  <p>“Why? Because of that stranger you met yesterday?” He grew angrier, his voice louder with each word. “You went to see him, alone in the dark. What is wrong with you, Fantah? That is not ladylike. <em>A wala pulaku na?</em>”</p>

  <p>She was about to reply angrily when Diddi appeared at the front door and told him their mother was calling for Fantah. They both walked back into the house, leaving Sa’adu fuming.</p>

  <p>As they were about to enter their mother’s hut, she appeared suddenly, and they froze in fear. She had been sleeping when Diddi went out; she must have woken to relieve herself.</p>

  <p>“Where are you coming from?”</p>

  <p>“Sa’adu came to see Fantah. So, we went to the frontage together to talk with him,” Diddi lied.</p>

  <p>“At this time of the night?”</p>

  <p>“Well, you know how wired he is.” Both Diddi and Fantah laughed nervously.</p>

  <p>“So, why didn’t he come in?”</p>

  <p>“He was coming from <em>hirgal</em> with his friends, so he stopped to talk about the <em>Sharo</em>. He seems really interested in going through with it for Fantah.” Fantah was both impressed and shocked by how easily and how well her sister could lie.</p>

  <p>“Ok then. You both go to bed now, now!”</p>

  <p>“Ok, mother,” they both replied.</p>

  <div id="page-8" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2017/03/15/10/44/ornate-room-2145824_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 8: Trouble</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
        
  <p>Princess Talatu was sitting in her beautiful room in Balda, accompanied by two of her servants, Purwa and Asakal. One was massaging her feet, and the other was braiding her hair. Her uncle, the Waziri, came to visit unannounced. As usual, his Gare bellowed angrily as he moved.</p>

  <p>"Salaamu Alaikum," he said and entered without waiting to be ushered in. Both girls knelt and turned their backs to him in respect, as it would have been considered rude for them to make eye contact with him.</p>

  <p>Talatu also came down from her bed and knelt to greet him. "Leave us!" He ordered and the two girls scurried out of the room like frightened kittens. Talatu did not even raise her head.</p>

  <p>"So, what has been going on? What can you tell me?" He asked.</p>

  <p>"Nothing, Uncle. He was still unhappy about the defeat," she replied, timidly.</p>

  <p>"I know that. But did he say anything?"</p>

  <p>"No. In fact, I haven't seen him for two days now."</p>

  <p>"You need to give me something I can work with, child! Your husband is the Lamdo's right-hand man and my route to the throne. Do not just sit here and act dumb."</p>

  <p>Talatu was offended, but there was nothing she could do. He was her uncle, and she loved him. She had always seen him as a kind man who was very rational. She had admired him for loving and respecting his brother, the king, even though he was older than him. But now, she worried about him. He asked her to spy on her husband and report his every move to him. She wasn't happy about it, and there were so many things wrong with spying on her husband that she disliked.</p>

  <p>As if he could read her mind, he said, "I know you think what I am doing is wrong, but you should understand that I am only helping you to secure our family name. Your husband is a stranger, and my brother, dumb as he is, put him in a position of power and shared all our secrets with him. I am just trying to ensure that he is not misusing that position, or worse, trying to usurp the throne from us and even kill our king. I am sorry, but I do not trust him that much."</p>

  <p>"I know, uncle. I really am doing my best to get something out of him. But for some reasons, he doesn't trust me. He seems distracted. He is always at the palace, and sometimes I don't see him for days."</p>

  <p>"Well, he is not at the palace right now, or anywhere else that I know of," he paused for a while. "He is up to something; I don't know what. But I will find out, I promise. In the meantime, I will send the Magajiya with something for you to give him. Make sure he eats it."</p>

  <p>"What is it?" She was worried.</p>

  <p>"Do not question me, child! You know I will never do anything to harm you. It is just a little belal bmandu that will give you an edge. You said he did not seem interested in you, well, after he eats it, you will be the only thing he thinks of. Now, tell me you don't want that," she didn't say anything. "Ok then, now that we have that settled, I will be leaving. Goodbye."</p>

  <p>"Goodbye, uncle," she said quietly.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Back at Ndokula, Malam Buba called his two daughters into his room for a talk. His wife was sitting by his side. He told them about the impending festival of Sharo. It is the yearly courtship festival in which young unmarried men of the Bororo clans undergo different types of dances and even torture to attract females for marriage. It is customary for all young men and women, and even though some parents do promise their daughters' hands in marriage without their consent, they still allow them to participate in the festival. If a maiden chooses a mate there, then the first betrothal is cancelled. Such is the significance of the event. All young girls look forward to it to decide on their choice of a partner. Even the young men look forward to it too. It is a matter of pride for their families to watch their sons endure the pains of whips gracefully, without showing any signs of fear. The benefits are instantaneous: fame, respect, and above all, a pretty wife to go with it.</p>

  <p>The festival was to be held in three months' time, and Malam Buba wanted to ensure they were prepared. His brother had come in respect of his son's interest in Fantah, and he needed to tell her about it. Murtala's parents also informed him of their son's interest in Diddi. So, he told them that he had approved both unless they have reservations. Unknown to him, Fantah wanted so badly to tell him about her stranger, but she was afraid to. She needed Hayah to show commitment first, to come and see her father, preferably together with some elders. That way, if she objects to marrying Sa'adu, she will have a very good reason for doing so, and her father would not be offended. She said a silent prayer, hoping he would keep to his word and not disappoint her this time.</p>

  <div id="page-9" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1500382017468-9049fed747ef?ixlib=rb-1.2.1&auto=format&fit=crop&w=1350&q=80')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 9: The Final Blow</h1>
        <p class="subtitle">Strike While the Iron is Hot!</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
        
  <p>Hayah was unhappy. Even the visit to see his beloved the previous day did nothing to uplift his spirit. He had been avoiding the Lamdo, who was upset with him over the recent incident. Not only did he fail him woefully, but he also lost a lot of his soldiers in the process, including two of his lieutenants, Umaru and Bawuro. The kingdom at that moment was vulnerable to attack and in dire need of military fortifications.</p>

  <p>There was also the issue of his proposal to Fantah. How should he approach the Lamdo about the issue? Was it even the right thing to do, given that he was married to his daughter? Nonetheless, he needed his blessings before he went ahead with the marriage. But that was not a good time to tell him. He would have to wait for an opportune time before going to him with the issue.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>In the past two months, Hayah had been deliberating with his remaining two lieutenants, Adamu and Arabu, on what to do. They all agreed that the Habe had made a strong statement and they needed to make an even stronger one to maintain their position of power over them. Lessons had been learnt on both sides. The Habe tribes had learned to cooperate, especially in war, and it was a bad omen for the Fulani. If they didn't put a stop to it, the Habe might become too strong for them to handle.</p>

  <p>They decided the best strategy was to go on the offensive, a surprise attack to disorient them and stop whatever they were planning before it had time to grow. And so, the following week, he sent a few of the dark skinned young Fulbe men to dress like the Habe and go into their communities in the mountains to spy on them. That way, they would know their exact locations and how to deal with them. They would also find out if there were any other surprises waiting for them. He had learned never to underestimate them.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Armed with the new and vital intelligence, Hayah planned to attack them at night. Failure was not an option for him. Not only was he desperate to get back into the Lamdo's good graces, but he had also tasted failure in the past, and he didn't like it. In fact, he had come here to correct the wrongs that were done to him back home. His father was bypassed for the throne due to a mistake he made, and it was given to his uncle. Because of that, he was disqualified from any aspirations to the throne of the Sultan of Sokoto. He could only secure the minor position of Sarking Gwandu, and even that was taken away from him when he was forced to abdicate.</p>

  <p>He left Sokoto in shame and headed east. Along the way, he heard of a very powerful king named El-Obeid in Sudan, who ironically was referred to as a Savior by his people. He thought the king might be of help to his cause. If he was to regain his lost throne, he needed all the help he could get, especially a powerful ally with the resources and a well-trained army to defeat his enemies. Sokoto was a very powerful kingdom, which required an equally powerful counterforce to defeat it, and the Mahdi seemed to fit the bill.</p>

  <p>He had been corresponding with the king secretly for several months, but shortly after got to Yola, the king died, forcing him to change his plans. He didn't receive the support he wanted from the Lamido of Yola, so in the interim, he proceeded Eastwards. However, when he reached Maroua, an opportunity presented itself. They needed military help, and he proved himself. That was how he got to where he was, and his plan was progressing smoothly until then.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>They waited until after dark, then started climbing into the mountainous territory very slowly and quietly, hiding behind the rocks and placing themselves at strategic points to wait for the villagers to retire to bed. At about three in the morning, when they were satisfied that everyone in the Habe settlements was asleep, they attacked. They descended on them with a sudden fury that took them by surprise. They broke down doors and killed any males they found in the huts. They took the women, children, and elderly into captivity. Some were given the charge of setting fire to the straw roofs, while others were looting, for bounty. The Habe didn't know what hit them. They were running helter-skelter and were being hacked down as they ran. The determined Fulani soldiers made sure they left no route for escape. The very few Habe who got away ran deeper into the mountains. Some of the soldiers wanted to follow them, but Hayah ordered them not to as it was too dangerous. They killed about eighty percent of the able-bodied men in the several settlements they attacked and took their women and children away. Only a handful of survivors were able to escape. It was pointless to risk following them. They would not be able to reorganize and come back for them for a long time. And when they finally do, the Fulbe will be more than ready for them.</p>

  <p>The Lamdo was very happy with the turn of events and elevated Hayah to a higher position in his court, with several celebrations held in his honor. There were three days of feasting and jubilation in Maroua, and everyone was happy. Even his wife got to see more of him, and she was happy.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>After the festivities, Hayah decided it was time to tell the king about the proposal. He waited until everyone left, but the Waziri refused to leave. In fact, since he came back, the Waziri had been hanging around the Lamdo a lot, as if he suspected something. Hayah couldn't tell him to leave. He was the Lamdo's brother after all. So, when he couldn't wait any longer, he decided to blurt it out. The look of surprise on the Waziri's face was priceless.</p>

  <p>"Wow, Imam. This is good news indeed," The Waziri said.</p>
  <p>"Yes, it is." The Lamdo also said. "You are a royal and should marry more,"</p>
  <p>"Talatu is going to have a sister," the Waziri laughed nervously.</p>

  <p>The Lamdo gave his blessing. And they decided that Hayah and the Waziri should be accompanied by two other royals to go to Ndokula for the induction ceremony in three days' time. As soon as Hayah got home, He sent a messenger to Ndokula to relay the message to Fantah.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>The next day, the Waziri waited for Hayah at the palace, but as soon as he arrived, the Waziri excused himself to go and see Talatu privately.</p>

  <p>"I told you he was up to something. Look at it now; this woman seems to consume him. Stop deluding yourself that you have the advantage because you are a princess. He loves her. And when a man loves a woman, she has a hold on him. Soon, you will be powerless in this house. Is that what you want?" He was very angry.</p>

  <p>"But uncle, what can I do? I can't tell him not to marry her," she protested.</p>

  <p>"Well, for starters, you can give him a son or several. That way, you know you have secured the kingdom for yourself. God help you if this woman comes here and delivers a son before you do." She did not say anything. "Have you been taking those concoctions that I gave you?"</p>

  <p>"Yes, uncle," she answered.</p>

  <p>"What about the one for him, has he taken it yet?"</p>

  <p>"I put it in his food... but he has not been eating here lately..."</p>

  <p>"Enough! I will send the Magajiya with more potions. Make sure you do it right this time." With that, he stormed out.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>That night, Hayah had a heart-to-heart talk with his wife. He took time to explain to her gently why he had to marry Fantah, and since she was a princess, she understood the many reasons why a prince hardly stays with only one wife.</p>

  <p>"Allah rene, you don't have to explain to me. Whatever you do is fine with me. I respect your wisdom," she said gently, trying to hide the disappointment in her voice.</p>

  <p>"You have every right to be upset. No one wants to share their spouse with anyone. But you should know that as the first wife and princess, you will always be esteemed above her," he said, trying to console her.</p>

  <p>"Allah rene, mi yetti." She spoke.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>The next day, Hayah went around to get the items they needed for the journey. According to Fulani custom, during the introduction to the bride's family, the groom's people will come along with and present to the girl's parents at least a bag of salt and a bag of kola nuts, both scarce commodities in those days. He prepared two donkey loads of both salt and kola nuts. To that, he added bags of rice and maize, twenty live chickens, and twenty geese.</p>

  <p>He wanted to make a powerful statement. He wasn't just any ordinary suitor but a powerful prince.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>While Hayah was out and busy preparing for the journey, Magajiya, an old woman who was responsible for the princess' well-being and previously her nanny while growing up, came to see her. She was sent by the Waziri to bring Talatu the items that he promised her.</p>

  <p>"Add this one to your bath water and put this one in your meals. Make sure you eat everything, no leftovers, and do not share with anyone!" she explained to the princess. "This one you give him to eat." She handed some dates to her. The princess gave them a skeptical look. "His Highness told me that you were unsuccessful in getting the prince to eat his 'special food.' Well, it is always the most difficult part. So, we have decided that the dates will be better. He can eat them even when he is not hungry. They are his favorite after all.</p>

  <p>"Yes, Magajiya. They are," she couldn't hide the pain and sadness in her voice.</p>

  <p>"Make sure he eats them, my princess. These things work; believe me." The Magajiya sensed her reluctance. "You have no idea what we went through to get them. If you want to maintain your position in this house, you have to be proactive. Love and feelings are for poor people and those who lack ambition. Forget about love and focus on protecting and situating yourself."</p>

  <p>"Okay. I will. I promise."</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Later that night, Hayah returned home exhausted but satisfied with his outing. He ate in her room, and after dinner, she presented him with the dates in a beautifully carved calabash.</p>

  <p>But she was very nervous. He picked one of them to eat, but just as he was about to put it in his mouth, he noticed that she was shaking like a leaf, and he dropped it.</p>

  <p>"Be careful, princess," he said quietly but authoritatively. "You cannot stop me. And if you try to hurt me, you will only end up hurting yourself." With that, he got up and left to spend his night elsewhere, away from her.</p>

    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2010/12/13/10/22/asian-2752_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 10: Spectacle</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>

    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
        
  <p>The next day, Hayah, the Waziri, the Chiroma, and more than a dozen servants - including musicians, town criers, and announcers - embarked on a journey to Ndokula to seek Fantah's hand in marriage. They carried along with them gifts and items that would be talked about for generations to come, both in Maroua and Ndokula. As they proceeded, people couldn't help but stop and stare in wonder.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Meanwhile, in Ndokula, Fantah's people were fully prepared and nervously awaiting the visitors. Malam Buba had invited his brother and brother-in-law to receive the guests. His brother was disappointed because he had hoped for his son, Sa'adu to marry Fantah, but he had no authority over the matter. Despite his feelings, he accepted the invitation and attended the gathering. The Ardo of the village was also invited, considering it was a royal matter, and he honored them with his presence. This display of respect elevated Malam Buba's status in the eyes of his townspeople who previously undervalued him due to having only daughters and no sons.</p>

  <p>In the patriarchal society of the Bororo, males held greater importance than females. Consequently, those with male children had more influence in society. Nonetheless, Malam Buba resisted societal pressures to take another wife. He knew it would upset his current wife, and that was something he never intended to do. Instead, he raised his daughters with care, treating them with the same attention as boys. While they exhibited ladylike and shy behavior when necessary, they were also strong, independent, and opinionated, akin to boys. This occasionally got them into trouble, but he paid no heed to society's judgment.</p>

  <p>However, on that day, everything was about to change. The tables had turned in his favor and he suddenly became the most important person in the village, all thanks to his daughter. Never before had he felt such immense pride.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Fantah sat nervously in her mother's hut, accompanied by her sister and mother, along with the wives of her uncles and their daughters. She was adorned in a beautiful outfit, black hand-woven wrappers adorned with white threads. Her hair was artfully braided, with a prominent bone rolled up above her forehead. Even when veiled, a part of her bun remained visible. Her makeup was flawlessly done, with a few white dots tracing her nose and cheeks. Adding to her sophistication, she wore her mother's jewelry.</p>

  <p>"I am sure that when he sees you, he will insist on marriage immediately," teased Diddi and they all laughed.</p>

  <p>"I wonder what he looks like," asked one of her cousins.</p>

  <p>"Oh, he's so handsome, you should see him," Diddi replied.</p>

  <p>Fantah blushed and remained shyly silent, maintaining her smile.</p>

  <p>"Really?"</p>

  <p>"Yes. He is tall and well-built. He also wears fine clothes."</p>

  <p>"You girls have no shame, do you?" scolded her aunt. "You are not only looking at men, but you even talk about them in our presence." Despite the scolding, they continued giggling and whispering.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>A few moments later, the suitor's entourage arrived, and Fantah's heart skipped a beat. The moment Hayah's group reached the village, the townspeople started coming out of their huts, awestruck by the sight. The entourage entered the Ndokula with great fanfare, dressed to impress and even their horses were adorned in colorful decorations. A musician played the Algaita, and the town crier announced their arrival and purpose for coming to Ndokula. The entire village was buzzing with excitement and curiosity.</p>

  <p>The villagers had never seen anything like it, and some decided to follow them to their destination. Upon arriving at Malam Buba's house, the town crier announced their arrival once again, and Sa'adu's father came out to receive them. They entered the compound and met with Malam Buba and his family. Pleasantries were exchanged, and then they stated the purpose of their visit and presented the gifts, leaving everyone impressed.</p>

  <p>"Well, we have shown you our hand; we would like to see yours," the Waziri said sarcastically, leaving Malam Buba confused.</p>

  <p>"What my uncle means to say is that we would like to see the bride-to-be," Hayah quickly interjected, shooting the Waziri a warning look.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Fantah was then brought out to meet the visitors. They lifted her veil, and even then, her head remained tilted down. One of her aunts gently lifted her head so they could see her face better.</p>

  <p>"Ahh! Vodi, Vodi!" everyone exclaimed, and Hayah's head swelled with pride.</p>

  <p>The Waziri felt a pang of jealousy; he hadn't expected such beauty. Now, more than ever, he wanted to destroy Hayah. After the fanfare, they agreed on a date for the wedding, three months later, two weeks after the Sharo ceremony, which was significant in the Bororo community. Hayah was not expected to participate in the Sharo but needed to show respect for the Bororo traditions and wait until after the event.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>After that was settled, Hayah sought permission from Malam Buba to take Fantah back to Maroua with him so that he could introduce her to the Lamdo. It wasn't necessary, but he wanted to do it out of respect for his master. Since the Lamdo couldn't go to Ndokula to see her, she would have to go to him. He gave Malam Buba his word to protect his daughter with his life, and everyone present became a witness. Malam Buba agreed to let Fantah go with them but did not allow Diddi to accompany her. He couldn't afford to part with both of his daughters at the same time.</p>

  <p class="chapter-end">[End of Chapter Ten]</p>

      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 11: Balda</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
       
  <p>The journey to Balda lasted about half a day on horseback, leaving Fantah exhausted from the long ride, as she was not accustomed to riding horses. However, the joy of being close to Hayah, holding on to him as they rode, made the fatigue seem insignificant. She could feel his warmth enveloping her like a comforting blanket, resting her head on his back, listening to the rhythmic thumping of his heartbeat as he breathed. As he shared stories of the battles he had fought and the bravery of his soldiers, she felt an immense sense of pride, knowing she was about to marry such a valiant warrior. The anticipation of the upcoming wedding filled her heart with excitement.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Upon their arrival in Balda early that afternoon, they were greeted by a quiet and peaceful town with a small population. Hayah's house, situated in the town's center, was adjacent to the central mosque, where the Chief Imam, known as the Ladan, was calling for the afternoon prayer. As a respected leader and teacher, it was essential for him to reside close to the mosque.</p>

  <p>Hayah's residence was grand and imposing, constructed with red mud bricks, and enclosed by a large surrounding wall. From outside, only the rooftops were visible, giving an air of mystery and privacy.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>As Hayah approached his house, the people of Balda respectfully knelt to greet him. He acknowledged them with a wave and led Fantah into one of the spacious apartments within the house. Here, she would rest and refresh herself before meeting the Lamdo the following morning. The interior of the house was equally impressive, comprising various apartments for different residents, ranging from Hayah, his wife, and concubines, down to the workers and servants. Each apartment's size and appearance reflected the occupant's status and importance.</p>

  <p>"Where will you be?" she asked, her voice filled with fear.</p>
  <p>"I will be in my apartment. I need to wash and change," he reassured her, noticing the fear in her eyes. "Don't worry, you will be fine. They will take good care of you."</p>
  <p>"Will I see you later?"</p>
  <p>"I will come to see you later. I promise."</p>
  <p>"Okay," she finally smiled.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>One of his hefty-looking guards approached them and assisted her in dismounting from the horse. He then took her small luggage into the house, while two beautiful servants guided her to her assigned room.</p>

  <p>Inside the apartment, Fantah was captivated by its splendid decorations. The beautiful wooden door bore impressive carvings, and the long corridor leading to her room was adorned with murals and calligraphy in Arabic. As they entered the large room, she felt the softness of the Kashmir carpet beneath her feet and was astonished by its beauty and comfort. She hesitated, afraid to step on something so exquisite, but a courtier noticed her reaction, smiled, and reassured her before guiding her further.</p>

  <p>The room was adorned with cushions arranged in an elegant pattern on the carpet. A table with flowers and vases added to the enchanting ambiance. The large windows were draped with thick curtains held open by golden ribbons, revealing white lace blinds. Fantah stood there, mouth agape, trying to take in the luxurious surroundings. Her attendants allowed her a few moments to absorb the grandeur before leading her to the inner room, which was slightly smaller than the first.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>In that room, she saw a remarkably large and beautiful bed, adorned with the most exquisite covers she had ever seen. In her hometown, they slept on raffia woven beds, simple and folded during the day. This bed was unlike anything she had ever encountered in her life.</p>

  <p>One of the female attendants led her to it and politely asked her to sit down. She was afraid at first, but the girl nudged her gently. When she sat down, she sank into the soft feathery cushion and instinctively jumped, thinking she was drowning. The girls laughed and assured her that it was okay, gently pulling her back onto the cushion.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>It took her a while to get accustomed to her new environment and relax. When her attendants noticed this, they brought her some food to eat. Afterward, they helped her out of her clothes and showed her to the bathroom. She was reluctant at first because she was not used to others seeing her naked, but after a lot of persuasion, she finally gave in.</p>

  <p>The bathroom was another tastefully decorated room. Back home, their bathrooms were detached and situated outside, surrounded by woven dakkwol for privacy. But this one looked like a living room. The walls were decorated, and in the center of the room, there was a large calabash filled with water, with some sweet-smelling herbs floating on it, along with a small bowl filled with black soap, a basket containing a natural loofah, and a wooden stool beside it.</p>

  <p>They pointed to the stool, and she sat down gently. Then one of the girls took a small calabash, fetched some water from the larger one, and poured it on her bare back. It felt warm, and she inhaled the sweet herbal fragrance. Slowly, she closed her eyes as she received her first royal bath.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>Afterward, she was dressed up in beautiful robes that Hayah had bought from Arab traders in preparation for her eventual arrival. She was treated like a queen by her new chaperones, who took the act of pampering to new heights. The change in appearance was dramatic, and she looked different from the shy nomadic girl who had just arrived moments ago. She was transformed into a beautiful princess adorned with golden jewelry dangling from each ear, her eyes glazed with excitement.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>At twilight, Hayah went to see her. She was in the visitor's room, seated on one of the large cushions, with one of the girls fanning her while the other was gently massaging her legs and telling her fables. Even though she protested at first, they were so persuasive that she gave in.</p>

  <p>"Salaamu Alaikum," he said as he stepped into the room. She looked up, and he had to catch his breath as she was even more beautiful than when he first saw her. She smiled and lowered her head.</p>
  <p>"Alaikum salaam," she answered. The two servants bowed, greeted him, then got up and went outside.</p>
  <p>"Wow. You are a beauty," he finally said when he came to, and she smiled again then looked away. He walked over and sat on another cushion next to her. "I see you have been taken care of."</p>
  <p>"Yes, thank you, Allah rene."</p>
  <p>"Don't call me that."</p>
  <p>"Ok stranger," she said, timidly and turned her gaze away in the other direction. He smiled and touched her face, turning her towards him. She smiled again and covered her face with her hands.</p>
  <p>"So, what do you think of my house?"</p>
  <p>"It is very beautiful."</p>
  <p>"It will soon be yours too." Fantah smiled again. "Have you eaten?"</p>
  <p>"Yes, I have. They were very kind to me."</p>
  <p>"Good." He leaned his elbow on one of the cushions. "I would like to introduce you to the lady of the house..." Fantah's heart started beating rapidly. "...If you are ready for it."</p>
  <p>"Of course, it would be rude of me not to go and greet her."</p>
  <p>He sat up and held her hand gently. "It's going to be all right. She is very nice."</p>
  <p>"Ok."</p>

  <hr />

  <p>He took her to another big apartment located in the sprawling compound. The door was similar to that of her apartment, but the color and decorations were different. There were also more servants in this one than there were at hers. As they entered the living room, all the servants knelt to greet them, then got up and left the room. Talatu, the lady of the house, was leaning on one of the mini beds. She got up and knelt to greet her husband, and then turned to Fantah.</p>

  <p>"Welcome," she said.</p>
  <p>Fantah also knelt and greeted her. "Thank you," she replied.</p>
  <p>"How are you?"</p>
  <p>"I am fine. I believe you had a wonderful trip."</p>
  <p>"Yes, I did, thank you." The tension in the room was so heavy; one could cut through it with a knife, literally.</p>

  <p>Hayah motioned her to a seat and then sat beside her. Talatu kept a pleasant smile on her face. If she was jealous, she didn't show it. She was a princess who had been trained all her life to maintain her poise and behave in a ladylike manner, no matter the situation.</p>

  <p>One of the chaperons brought some refreshments, and then another one brought the food. Fantah prayed a silent prayer, hoping they wouldn't eat together, but the prayer must have gone unanswered as Hayah was intent on creating a bond between them. During the meal, he told Talatu about Fantah's wonderful village, and to Fantah, how Talatu grew up as a princess. Both women said very little, trying their best to be polite and pretending to be having fun. It was the most difficult meal of Fantah's life.</p>

  <hr />

  <p>After dinner, he escorted her back to her apartment. At the door, he stopped to apologize to her.</p>

  <p>"I know you were not expecting this, but I had to."</p>
  <p>"It's ok," she lied, smiling.</p>
  <p>"I care about you, Fantah, and I don't want you to have any problems here."</p>
  <p>"Ok. But..." She stopped.</p>
  <p>"But, what?" He asked.</p>
  <p>"If it's all right with you 'stranger,' I would like to have my meals at my own apartment from now on."</p>
  <p>He gave a little laugh. "I know. I noticed how little you ate. I promise I will never put you through that again."</p>
  <p>"Thank you."</p>
  <p>"I have informed the Lamdo of your arrival, and I will take you to meet him in the morning."</p>
  <p>"I will be ready at first light."</p>
  <p>He pats her cheek gently. "Have a good night's rest, my love."</p>
  <p>"And you, my stranger."</p>

    <p>After breakfast the next morning, Hayah took an elegantly dressed Fantah on a short journey to Maroua to finally meet with the Lamdo. This time, they rode on different horses. One of his trusted guards was given the task of harnessing her horse to prevent it from galloping too fast. It made Fantah feel a lot better as she had never ridden alone before. They also had two other armed escorts.</p>

    <p>Looking at her, no one could tell if she slept the previous night or not, and she didn't. She was too nervous to. All kinds of thoughts about the journey were running through her mind. To begin with, she had never met any Lamdo before. So, she wouldn't know how to comport herself in his presence. To make matters worse, he was Talatu's father. She didn't know if he would be happy to meet her given that she was about to marry his son-in-law.</p>

As they rode through the streets of the city, the townspeople recognized him and were kneeling to greet him as a mark of respect, and he smiled and waved at them. It suddenly began to dawn on her that Hayah was a very important man, indeed.</p>

    <p>The palace of the Lamdo Garei was a sight to behold. It flabbergasted her. The walls were huge and covered with colorful murals and writings. There were four huge domes, one on each corner of the wall. The tip of each dome was golden in color and shone beautifully in the sun. The entrance was huge and lined with giant, well-built guards who were armed to the teeth and dressed in red outfits, with black turbans on their heads.</p>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>To be within the palace was like being transported into another universe. As they rode farther into the palace, it appeared massive and honeycombed with different stand-alone apartments. The Lamdo's apartment was the largest and most elaborate one, located close to the entrance, followed by his administrative quarters where he meets and interacts with his subjects.</p>

    <p>The Chiroma's wife, as her first hostess, took her further into the palace to greet all the Lamdo's four wives, starting with the eldest, whose apartment was the least elaborate of the four but where she felt the most welcomed. The woman, fondly called Dada, was so warm and welcoming.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>At the entrance to the Lamdo's chamber, the Kofa loudly announced their presence before they were ushered in. They went into a large oval parlor, which was earthy, red in color, and furnished with rugs, carpets, and curtains of various shades of red and white.</p>

    <p>The Lamdo sat on a large wooden throne, dressed in an elaborate silver alkibba and a white turban wrapped around his head and neck. He was also holding a white and silver staff of office. There were many noblemen in the king's court, dressed in different colourful babban rigas, who were all seated on the sprawling rug in a semi-circle formed around the Lamdo's throne.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Later that night, at midnight, Talatu was in her apartment when her uncle, the Waziri, suddenly walked in unannounced. Her servants scurried away while she quickly tried to compose herself.</p>

      <p>"Uncle! I had no idea you were coming."</p>
      <p>"I know." He replied. "I just came from the Lamdo's palace." He paused a little for dramatic effect. "Do you know that he gave his blessing to Hayah and his new concubine?" She didn't say anything. "It is obvious he loves her very much. Anyone can see that, and after he marries her, where will you be?"</p>
    </div>
  </div>

    <p>The next morning, Fantah was in her room with her two assigned female attendants when without warning the 'lady of the house' walked in, followed by two female attendants.</p>

    <div class="dialogue">
      <p>"What are you doing here, Fantah?" The princess asked her; it was more rhetorical than direct.</p>
      <p>"I don't follow."</p>
      <p>"You have no place here."</p>

    <div class="dialogue">
      <p>"Fantah!" He yelled her name. She turned around briefly, but as soon as she saw him, she started to run.</p>
      <p>"Listen to me. I am Fantah; the daughter of Buba Bunu, the great-granddaughter of Bunu Balewa. A free woman from the proud Bororo tribe of Ndokula, and I can never be your slave. So let go of me or my people will have your head for supper!"</p>
    </div>

    <p>He saw that her mind was made up, so he helped her to mount the horse, and they started towards Ndokula. On their way, they discussed things, especially their marriage plans. They reached the village late at night, but Hayah had a very important meeting with the chiefs in Maroua scheduled for the next day, and so, had to go back immediately.</p>

  <h2>Chapter Thirteen: Deceit</h2>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Talatu was worried. It had been two weeks since the incident with Fantah, and her husband had been avoiding her. He left home very early and arrived home late at night. He avoided any intimate contact and left his food untouched too. She knew he was upset about the whole episode, but never knew to what extent until it dawned on her that Fantah was not just any pretty girl, but one that meant a lot to her husband.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Very early the next morning, as her husband was about to mount his horse, she quickly ran to him, knelt, and held his leg, crying and begging for forgiveness.</p>

    <div class="dialogue">
      <p>"I am sorry, Allah rene. My jealousy got the best of me," Talatu said.</p>
      <p>"I promise I will never do anything of that nature again," she continued, looking at him earnestly. "Please, Allah rene, do not avoid your wife like that. It is not Islamic and certainly not befitting your status. I am begging you. Please forgive me."</p>
    </div>

    <p>He softened up, then pulled her up to stand. "Ok, I have heard you. Forgive me too. I promise I will come back to your bed again. But I need to go out now as I have some urgent work to do."</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>She also persuaded her uncle, the Waziri, to intercede on her behalf. Hayah was at the stalls, tending to his favorite horse when the Waziri arrived.</p>

      <p>"Salamu Alaikum, Allah rene," the Waziri greeted Hayah.</p>
      <p>"Alaikum Salaam, Waziri."</p>
      <p>"Allah sabbine. Allah Wadu Barka." He showered him with praises. "What can I do for you, Waziri?" Hayah was getting impatient. He could read him like a book, and he was aware that the Waziri was a two-timing snake.</p>
      <p>"I just had a little chat with my daughter..." He started, nervously. "She had been crying because she thought that you were angry with her. Hahaha. Silly girl... you know how women are. Ha ha...." He laughed again, nervously. "I told her that you could never be angry with her. She is your precious wife and could never do wrong in your eyes. Hahaha."</p>
      <p>"Actually, she could, and she did," Hayah said without looking at him.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>It was all well again. For the next three months, while waiting for the Sharo festival to take place, Hayah visited Fantah several times. Sometimes, he spent the night in their house, and at other times, his visits were brief.</p>

    <p>He loved the time he spent with her, and fortunately for him, there were no royal assignments for him to carry out at such times. Whatever threats existed, especially from the indigenous tribes, had been successfully neutralized, giving him time to relax and plan his marriage in detail.</p>

And this other one is called Bita zoi-zoi. Make sure you soak a little of each, mixed, and leave it for at least half a day. Then drink it. Afterward, make sure you have sex with him. I promise you, he will be all you will think of after that."</p>

  <div id="page-14" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2017/09/09/18/32/bracelets-2732974_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 14: Sharo</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
       
  <div class="scene">
    <p>It was the day of the Sharo, a day anticipated by both boys and girls in the Bororo community all year round. It was the day when boys became men, and maidens sought husbands. The Sharo involved a very painful ritual of coming of age. Once a year, the Bororo extended clans gathered in a predetermined village square where young boys hit each other on their bare backs with sticks in a show of strength, all the while laughing hysterically to hide any signs of pain.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>The girls prepared for the Sharo with equal zeal. They woke up early to cook. Afterwards, they applied fresh lalle on their hands and feet, washed and braided their hair, and applied generous amounts of Man Shanoo to it. Later, they were expected to sweep and decorate the whole village before proceeding to the stream to wash their bodies.</p>

    <p>Hayah arrived in the village around noon and decided to go and check on Fantah in the fields where she was supposed to be tending to the cows. But she wasn't there. He noticed the decorations in the village and the air of festivity.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>There were girls of different sizes and shapes taking their bath in the stream. He had never seen anything like that. They were so beautiful. He noticed Fantah by the side, exfoliating her feet with a stone. She was naked except for a piece of wet cloth wrapped around her waist. He had never seen her like that before.</p>

    <p>Just at that moment, one of the girls looked up and saw his face in the tree. She screamed loudly and pointed. The other girls looked up and saw him too. They also started screaming and then grabbed their cloths and scattered in every direction.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Later that day, the whole village gathered at the square for the event. The girls were all looking very beautiful and adorned, evidence of their intense preparedness. Some wore tiny, belly-baring tops with wrappers decorated with beads. Their hands, feet, and even hair were all decorated with beads of different colors and sizes.</p>

    <p>The men were in the middle of the square and were equally, if not more, flamboyantly dressed. They wore large trousers and sandals with no shirts on, only beads crisscrossed their chests. The girls loved men with flat tummies, as it was a sign that they were physically fit.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>The eligible bachelors all lined up in the middle while the maidens took their time to inspect them before selecting their potential husbands. The young men would stretch their hands forward, open their eyes wide, and display their teeth as much as possible.</p>

    <p>Fantah looked around the square but did not see Hayah among the boys. As she scanned the line-up of brave boys, her heart sank when she spotted Sa'adu among them. She knew why he was there, and she knew he would be expecting her to pick him, even though she was engaged to someone else.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Before the second and most important round, the whipping, the boys would rub all kinds of concoctions on their backs and drink all kinds of liquids prescribed to them by their own personal native doctors.</p>

    <p>Then, to everybody's surprise, Hayah also came out. Fantah was worried.</p>

    <div class="dialogue">
      <p>"Baba, what is he doing here?" A worried Fantah asked her father, who was standing next to her.</p>
      <p>"I guess he is about to get whipped," her father answered, sarcastically.</p>
      <p>"But Baba, he is not from here."</p>
      <p>"Well, if he wants to marry my daughter, he has to go through Sharo like everybody else."</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>Hayah began to receive his beating. The first whip came down hard, feeling like he was being branded with a hot iron. He tried to pretend not to feel it by smiling. The second one came down, and he felt the world around him beginning to spin. By the time the fifth stroke came down, the world was already spinning like a hurricane, and Hayah felt the earth give way under his feet, and then everything went black.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>When he woke up, he found himself on a bed in a familiar room with a sweet-smelling ointment smeared across his back. It was Fantah's father's hut. Just then, Fantah walked in holding a bowl of hot water and a piece of cloth.</p>

    <div class="dialogue">
      <p>"Sannu," he said.</p>
      <p>"Sannu 'Stranger', you are awake."</p>
      <p>"Is this heaven?" He asked.</p>
      <p>"That was so stupid," she said. "Why would you risk your life like that for nothing?"</p>
      <p>"I did it for you, my love."</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>During the next few days, the Buba family took good care of him. They gave him some concoctions to drink and rubbed ointments on his back. It worked wonders. Not only was he completely healed in a matter of days, but his scars were beginning to become less noticeable.</p>

    <p>While he was recuperating, he and Malam Buba got to talk a lot. They discussed his intentions concerning the marriage and what would become of Fantah. He gave Malam Buba his word that he had nothing to worry about. He loved Fantah with his life, and he hoped she would become his queen one day.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="scene">
    <p>After spending a total of twelve days in Ndokula, he was fully recovered and prepared to go home to get on with the preparations. He would come back in exactly one week with his people, to take his beautiful bride back to Balda. He couldn't wait.</p>
  </div>

  <p class="chapter-end">[End of Chapter Fourteen]</p>

     <!-- Chapter 15 -->
  <div id="page-15" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2024/12/28/03/31/green-tree-python-9295182_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 15: Snake!</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                <p>It was the D-day, and Hayah was ready to go to Ndokula for his formal marriage ceremony to Fantah. He had prepared a dream team of delegates, including the Chiroma and the Waziri, who, to his surprise, seemed to have approved of the whole arrangement.</p>
        
        <p>He presented forty gold coins for the dowry and twenty bales of the finest clothes materials brought from North Africa for his bride. He also presented twenty bags of rice, twenty bags of corn, twenty bags of millet, one herd of cattle, and ten big calabashes of honey as gifts for the family of his new bride. His wife Talatu also seemed excited and happy for him, especially considering what she recently did to him; she had been trying to make it up to him ever since. He gave his wife a lot of lavish gifts of lands, clothes, jewelry, and livestock, as required by tradition, in order to calm her down and as compensation for allowing him to take a second wife.</p>
        
        <p>The following day, at about nine o'clock in the morning, everything looked good, and everyone was set. "Nothing can stop me now," Hayah thought happily. It was at that very moment that a royal messenger came to inform Hayah and the Chiroma that the Lamdo of Maroua was very ill, and they rushed to Maroua to see him.</p>
        
        <p>Hayah wasn't particularly surprised by the news; he was just disappointed. The king had always been sick. He was very old and frail, and he knew that death was not too far away. But the timing couldn't have been more inconvenient. Unfortunately, there was nothing he could do. As the chief imam, he needed to be there to pray for him and be with him in his last moments.</p>
        
        <p>So, the journey to Ndokula for his marriage ceremony was canceled, and he went to be by the dying Lamdo's bedside at Maroua.</p>
        
        <p>Before leaving for Maroua, after dispatching his entourage, he quickly wrote a message to Fantah:</p>
        
        <div class="letter">
            <p>My dear Fantah,</p>
            
            <p>I pray this letter meets you well. I was on my way to Ndokula with my people for our marriage when I got the unfortunate news that his Majesty was very ill and in critical condition. As his Chief Imam, I have no choice but to go and attend to him. Therefore, the ceremony will have to be delayed. Kindly give my regards to your father. As soon as the king starts to feel better, we will come and fulfill the marriage rites, I promise.</p>
            
            <p>I have some gifts for you and your family. I hope you will like them.</p>
            
            <p>I cannot wait to be with you again, my beloved.</p>
            
            <p class="letter-signature">With all my love, Hayah.</p>
        </div>
        
        <p>He wrapped it in a beautiful skin scroll and sealed it, then gave it to a servant, who was also a student of his, to run to Ndokula and read it to Fantah immediately and also explain the situation to her family. Then he proceeded with the Chiroma to go to Maroua.</p>
        
        <p>"Where is the Waziri?" He asked him.</p>
        
        <p>"He had gone to inform his niece about the news," the Chiroma said.</p>
        
        <p>"Oh, I almost forgot about her. We are fortunate that he was here then."</p>
        
        <p>"Yes, we are. He said it may take a while as he would need to comfort her first. So, we should get going fast. He will meet us there as soon as he can."</p>
        
        <p>"Ok then, let us go," Hayah said, and they left in the company of a lot of servants and guards.</p>
        
        <p>The messenger that Hayah sent was leaving the compound through the servants' entrance when the Waziri intercepted him. He knelt to greet him.</p>
        
        <p>"Good day, young man," the Waziri said. "Where are you going in such a hurry?"</p>
        
        <p>"I have a very important message from my master for the lady, Fantah, and I have to hurry because they are waiting at Ndokula."</p>
        
        <p>"Ah yes. Let's have a talk," the Waziri replied.</p>
        
        <p>A few minutes later, he was at Talatu's apartment, holding the message in his hand.</p>
        
        <p>"Look, I intercepted it before the idiot left. It was not easy. I had to pay him an arm and a leg as a bribe. Now, Fantah and her family will look like fools in front of their whole village after waiting for us in vain. Knowing the Bororo with their short temper and humongous pride, I am sure that would be enough to make the father change his mind and give her to someone else to save face. Do not say that I never did anything for you."</p>
        
        <p>"Are you sure that he is not going to tell my husband?"</p>
        
        <p>"Of course, I settled him handsomely. Besides, if he does, it will implicate him as well, so he wouldn't dare!" He paused. "You don't look happy."</p>
        
        <p>"I am not happy, uncle; my father is ill, and he may be dying." Tears were rolling out of her eyes.</p>
        
        <p>"Yes, my daughter. It is sad. But do not worry; I am sure he is going to be alright. Listen to me." He moved close to her. "I need you to be really strong at this time, ok? A lot of things are about to change within a short time, and you cannot afford to show any kind of weakness. I did all this for you because I love you."</p>
        
        <p>She looked at him in disbelief, a chill running down her spine. Even without saying the words, she knew he was responsible for her father's illness. Of all the despicable things he had done, this was by far the most heinous. She was suddenly more afraid of him at that moment than she had ever been in her whole life.</p>
        
        <p><strong>[End of Chapter Fifteen]</strong></p>

  <div id="page-16" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2024/09/05/02/08/ai-generated-9023292_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 16: Shattered!</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                        <p>At the royal palace in Maroua, a select number of preachers and nobles were praying for the king's quick recovery. While a doctor treated him with herbs, he vomited all day. Some said it was poison, others claimed it was a jinn, but all were concerned he might not survive it, considering his age. Hayah and the Chiroma were by his bedside, feeding, cleaning, and praying for him. Intermittently, Hayah would join in reciting the Qur'an together with some of his students, and after each chapter, they would pray and continue. By midnight, there was still no improvement. In fact, the king's health had deteriorated steadily throughout the day, and Hayah knew the wait would not be long. By 4 am, the king gave up the ghost.</p>
        
        <div class="scene-divider">…………………………………..</div>
        
        <p>Meanwhile, in Ndokula, the mood was horrible. The Waziri was right. Malam Buba's family was highly embarrassed before their guests due to the no-show on the part of Hayah, and his people, and his absence of an explanation. They had prepared for the marriage ceremony and invited everyone from the village, and they all came out, dressed in their very best outfits. Meals were prepared, and chickens and cows were slaughtered. The slaughter of cows is a big deal in the Bororo community, reserved only for very special occasions, as cows are highly valued, almost like family members.</p>
        
        <p>Malam Buba was humble, but he couldn't resist the feeling of pride as his daughter was about to marry one of the most important men in the Fulani kingdom. And to think that she was the daughter he had the least hope in, gave him joy. Fantah had always been hot-headed and a rule-breaker, which had set tongues wagging in the village. He had been previously worried that no man in the village would marry her. Thankfully, his brother was willing to let his son marry her, and the boy seemed to like the idea. But when Hayah came along, he couldn't refuse him. Besides, she seemed to like him too, so he prayed that the marriage would be a happy one. As for his brother, he apologized to him and was forgiven. Well, enough of ruminating. It's D-day, and there is no going back.</p>
        
        <p>Earlier that morning, he had gone to his wife's hut to chat with her and his daughters. He was so happy and prayed for all of them. Diddi's marriage was to be held the next week to not interfere with Fantah's.</p>
        
        <p>But everything was shattered by the turn of events. They waited all day, but Hayah and his entourage never arrived. There was no message, no word, or explanation. They sat there looking like fools in front of everybody. They had made up all sorts of excuses that grew weirder and weirder as the day progressed. Around sundown, people started leaving one after the other, and by twilight, the house was completely empty of visitors. His wife and Diddi were in the room crying. No one knew where Fantah was, but he knew undoubtedly that she must have been crying her eyes out with hot tears wherever she was. As for Malam Buba, he was standing in the cowshed praying silently for the earth to open and swallow him. He dreaded the next morning when he will have to face his neighbors.</p>
        
        <div class="scene-divider">…………………………………..</div>
        
        <p>Fantah was standing in the middle of the field where she and Hayah used to meet. It was way past midnight, and she was alone in the fields, wondering what had happened. Despite all the embarrassment, she looked beautiful. She was wearing her late grandmother's wedding outfit, preserved through the years for her, and it fitted her elegantly. She was also wearing some of the expensive jewelry that Hayah had given her as a gift during her last visit to Balda. It was the first time she wore them. She waited for a special time to wear them, and what could be more special than her wedding day?</p>
        
        <p>The day had started on a wonderful note. Diddi made her hair, and her mother gave her a bath and did her make-up for her that morning. Her hands and feet were shiny black with lalle, and she smelled nice from the sweet oils and perfumes that were applied to her. Everything was perfect. The only thing missing was her prince. But at that moment, it felt like the whole world was sitting on her chest. She couldn't understand what had happened. Hayah loved her, and she knew it. He would never do anything to hurt her; he was a nobleman who cared about his reputation and would never do anything to sully his image. What could have happened? She wondered. Was he alright? Was he alive? Did his wife stop him? She wouldn't put it past her. Maybe she used charms and witch doctors to get him to change his mind. And now, he doesn't want her anymore. She just couldn't reconcile the whole thing in her mind.</p>
        
        <p>Her eyes were dry. She couldn't cry. She was still in shock. She tried to cry, to get some relief from the pain and sorrow in her heart, but the tears wouldn't just flow. She was too wired up to cry. So, she just stood there alone in the field in the middle of the night, looking and wondering.</p>
        
        <p>Somewhere behind tall grasses, Sa'adu was watching her. He was worried about her. He was afraid she might do something to hurt herself. So, he followed her without her knowledge. Earlier that day, he had gone to her and wished her a happy married life. She felt guilty, but he told her not to worry; he would always love her and look out for her. After all, she was still his sister. That was exactly what he was doing.</p>
        
        <div class="scene-divider">…………………………………..</div>
        
        <p>Meanwhile, in Maroua, the Lamdo's death hadn't been officially confirmed or announced yet, but there were whispers here and there, and the tension in the town was high. Hayah and the Chiroma met with the kingmakers on how to select a worthy successor as soon as possible. There were two royal families involved: the Garei and the Iya, both ascending the throne alternately. Over the years, the Iya family had grown weaker, while the Garei family grew stronger. The possibility of the Iya family regaining the throne at this moment was very small, indeed. The sudden death of the Lamdo had presented a serious problem for the kingmakers, and while they were at it, the Chiroma, who was from the Garei family, was already being taunted by many vested interests as the leading candidate. However, the Iya family also presented a candidate, and a power tussle ensued. There were fierce arguments, tempers flared, and threats were not scarce. A compromise seemed like the best solution to the conundrum. To cool flaring tempers, Hayah reminded them that the late monarch was not yet buried. In Islam, it is customary to bury the dead as soon as possible before matters of inheritance, which they were violating. After a few grumblings here and there, they all agreed to go and bury the Lamdo. An official announcement was made, and the city went into mourning. Not a few citizens attended the burial of their beloved monarch later that afternoon. As soon as the burial rites were over, the internal politics continued. They deliberated for hours until finally, by midnight, they agreed on the unlikely choice of Salli from the Iya family. The announcement was made at daybreak, and a coronation was scheduled for the next Friday.</p>
        
        <p>Everything was happening very fast, and Hayah barely had time to catch his breath. It was as if the universe was against him. His life was suddenly turned upside down. His marriage plans were ruined. He kept sending messages to Fantah but never got a reply. He didn't blame her. He had embarrassed her and her family in no small measure. Even though it was not his fault, he understood what they were going through. He wanted to go and see her to explain everything, but he couldn't. The Lamdo, his friend, and mentor were dead, and this new Lamdo did not seem to like him. He could feel it. The kingdom was also vulnerable to attacks by the Habe before the coronation when the Lamdo would have taken control of it. He had a feeling they would want to test the resolve of the new Lamdo. It didn't matter that they were recently dealt a heavy military blow. In fact, the anger and resentment might motivate them to attack. Something needed to be done to dissuade them from such a course of action - something big and scary - requiring resources and detailed preparations. And he was constrained to stay.</p>
        
        <p>That night, Hayah went home, tired, and sad. The Lamdo was not just his friend but was also like a father to him. He was his father-in-law, after all. His death really affected him, and yet, he couldn't even mourn him because of his position as the chief Imam. He went to Talatu's room. She was in a corner crying. He realized that she was in even more pain than he was. Not only had she just lost her own biological father, but the throne of the Lamdo was no longer in her immediate family. Her political relevance was greatly reduced. He felt so sorry for her and went over to comfort her.</p>
        
        <p>“I am sorry, my love. It is quite unfortunate what happened. But it was his time. We cannot argue with God's will.” She kept sobbing. He placed a hand on her shoulder. “I loved him too, you know. He was like a father to me. I am sorry.” All he wanted to do at that time was to comfort her and ease her pain. And he did.</p>
        
        <p>He made love to her that night, not just as a duty or to satisfy himself, but to make things better for both of them, to bring some peace and comfort, which they both desperately needed at that moment. What he didn't know was that she had earlier drunk the concoctions that the Magajiya gave her.</p>

  <div id="page-17" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2013/09/27/17/42/prince-palace-187379_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 17: The King Makers</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                               <p>The in-fighting between the two royal families continued. With the possible outbreak of a civil war hanging in the air, many vested interests were not satisfied with the choice of Salli as the new Lamdo of Maroua, including Hayah. However, as the highest-ranking military General, he held the kingdom in check and mediated between the royal families to accept the kingmaker's decision to save the kingdom from both internal and external turmoil.</p>
        
        <p>But despite his good intentions, the new Lamdo was suspicious of him. He saw Hayah's enthusiasm in trying to keep the kingdom together as a maneuver to usurp him. For him, it was mutiny. Not only was Hayah very powerful, but his followers had increased significantly since his arrival. When he settled at Balda, he came as a scholar with a few students and claimed that he only wanted to spread knowledge. But within a short time, he became the Lamdo's favorite, who also commanded the army. He had the potential of becoming a very serious threat to the kingdom. For some not-too-obvious reasons, the previous Lamdo trusted him very much. But he wasn't totally absolved from suspicion from the royals.</p>
        
        <p>To make matters worse for Hayah, the Waziri had already ingratiated himself and become the new Lamdo's confidant. As a matter of fact, it was his secret manipulations that brought Salli to power. Lamdo Salli was his candidate. And now that he was in a position of power, he was manipulating him to send Hayah out of the kingdom. However, he was clever not to push too hard. Instead, he pretended to have the best interest of the kingdom at heart, while filling Salli's head with lies and horrid pictures of the future of Balda if Hayah is left in charge. The Lamdo believed him.</p>
        
        <p>So, every time Hayah requested new equipment or new recruits of soldiers, the new Lamdo would object to it. He was always frustrated. He didn't know what to do. Even the intervention of his best friend, the Chiroma, did not better his lot. He later found out that Lamdo Salli had been consorting with other minor monarchs all around Fombina to have him exiled from the kingdom.</p>
        
        <p>Lamdo Salli then wrote a letter to the Lamido of Yola:</p>
        
        <div class="letter">
            <p>Allah sabbine,</p>
            <p>May the peace and blessings of Allah be upon you.</p>
            <p>I write to inform you of the threat facing my kingdom and I believe, Fombina as a whole, by the name Hayah. As you are aware, he came here on the pretext of being a teacher, but over the years his followers have increased greatly and have become more fanatical every day, and I worry that he will become a problem beyond me soon.</p>
            <p>I request that you order him back to Sokoto before he becomes too big of a problem for us to handle.</p>
            <p class="letter-signature">Lamdo Salli.</p>
        </div>
        
        <p>All the Lamdos in the kingdom of Fombina reported to the Lamido of Yola, Lamido Sanda, who was the supreme ruler of the kingdom, and Yola was the capital of Fombina. The Lamido replied to him:</p>
        
        <div class="letter">
            <p>Lamdo Salli,</p>
            <p>May the peace and blessings of Allah be upon you as well.</p>
            <p>I have read your letter, but I do not see the threat that you talked about. Hayah is the great-grandson of Usumanu the Great, and I will not be hasty to exile him from my kingdom because of your fears. Unless you have evidence to support your claims, I will not risk ruining the good name of my kingdom before the Sokoto Caliphate.</p>
            <p>In the meantime, he will stay as long as it pleases him.</p>
            <p class="letter-signature">Lamido Sanda.</p>
        </div>
        
        <p>Lamdo Salli was disappointed.</p>
        
        <p>After that incident, Hayah realized that he was in danger and moved quickly. To achieve his plans, he needed to find a more powerful benefactor. So, he sent his most trusted lieutenant, Arabu, with a letter to his powerful ally, King Mohammed Ahmed of Sudan, also known as 'Muharib' by his people, pledging allegiance to him. In return, the Muharib sent him a reply, effectively appointing him as Deputy Amirul Muminin, to exercise authority over all Muslims under Fulani rule, including the Lamido and the Lamdos of Fombina. He also sent a letter to the Sokoto Caliphate, informing them of his decision and instructing them to submit to Hayah on all Islamic matters. All the letters carried the official emblem of the Muharib and the royal seal of the kingdom of Sudan. It also came with a flag, which Hayah was to carry as the Amirul Muminin. Hayah's cousin, Umaru, who was the Sultan of Sokoto at that time, felt insulted by the letter and refused to submit. "How dare him ask the Caliphate to submit to that idiot?" He fumed and sent a defiant reply to him. Not only was the kingdom of Sokoto powerful enough to withstand any external attack, but it also had the Europeans as new allies. The Europeans had already started their colonization of Africa and were slowly annexing lands through fraudulent trade treaties with traditional rulers. The ones that went to Sokoto were the British who had very sophisticated weapons and battle-thirsty soldiers, ready to defend their 'newly found ally' if need be. Hayah's plan of using the Muharib of Sudan failed woefully.</p>
        
        <p>Days turned into weeks, and then months as Hayah fought to maintain his position. He never stopped preaching, and despite the Sokoto Caliphate's denouncement, others didn't follow suit. In fact, his declaration as the new Deputy Amir increased his followers in great numbers. He amassed so many supporters that he built a new mosque that rivaled that of Yola. He also commissioned the construction of a new market to improve the lives of the people of Balda. At first, the Lamdo refused to provide him with the necessary funds, but after convincing him of the project's tremendous positive effect on Maroua's economy, the Lamdo reluctantly gave him only half of the requested amount. Nevertheless, this setback didn't discourage Hayah; instead, he utilized the many captured slaves from his recent military campaigns and his devoted followers as cheap labor. Before long, the market and the mosque were completed, leading to an even larger increase in his followers.</p>
        
        <p>There was further good news. His wife gave birth to a bouncing baby boy whom he named Sai'du after his father. He cherished the boy deeply and took him along everywhere he went. However, he had hoped Fantah would be the one to give him an heir. Even though he couldn't go to see her, he never ceased thinking of her. He wrote several letters, unaware that his messengers were not delivering them in the first place. The messengers, who he never knew about, told him that Fantah looked unhappy. Hayah knew she was still hurting from the embarrassment he caused her and her family, and he acknowledged that she had every right to be angry. He had attempted to go and apologize, but he was always constrained by circumstances that seemed to deliberately prevent him from seeing her. There was a powerful force holding him back, one he could not see and didn't understand. Nonetheless, he never stopped loving her and thinking about her, wondering where she was, what she was doing, and if she had married someone else. The silence was unbearable, and he found himself yearning for her, saying, "Oh my beloved, Fantah."</p>
        
        <p><strong>[End of Chapter Seventeen]</strong></p>

        <h1>Chapter 18: The Plan</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                                       <p>Back at Ndokula, Fantah had waited three long years for any word from her stranger, but none came. The last time he came to see her, he promised the whole family he would soon be back. They prepared for the wedding and waited in vain for him to return. It was an embarrassment that could not be quantified. At first, the villagers pretended to ignore everything, out of courtesy and respect for Malam Buba. However, as time passed, the whispers grew louder.</p>
        
        <p>Fantah felt uncomfortable in her own skin, living in that village. Even her own father was against her. He was always yelling at her and making hurtful comments like, <span class="dialogue">"That is why you are still not married"</span> or <span class="dialogue">"You are too old to be living here with us."</span></p>
        
        <p>A year after the embarrassing incident, they got tired of waiting and married Diddi off to her fiancé, Murtala, who had braved the Sharo for her. It made things worse for Fantah. The villagers couldn't stop talking and pointing fingers. She had watched, with mixed feelings, all her peers getting married, then their younger sisters, and even their younger sisters' younger sisters. It was embarrassing and soul-crushing. She also witnessed them giving birth to children while she remained single and grew old, waiting for Hayah.</p>
        
        <p>Malam Buba could not even attend village meetings or any other gatherings with his mates because someone was always making hurtful comments about what happened to his daughter. He was embarrassed and ashamed, which naturally turned him against her. And that evening, he had had enough.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Fantah, you better bring someone to marry you, or else I will force you to marry whomever I want!"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Father, Hayah said he will return..."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Shut up!"</span> He cut her off. <span class="dialogue">"That useless man could not even honor his promise. He took my daughter for a ride and then abandoned her. That is not a very royal behavior, is it? You have brought shame on this family."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Father, it's not like that..."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I said, shut up. You have one week to bring someone, or I will announce in the village square that I am giving you out to anyone interested, for free!"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"You cannot do that, Father!"</span> She snapped at him.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Shut up!"</span> He gave her a hard slap on the face, and she fell backward, her head reeling from the pain. Her mother came running out from the hut to hold her and comfort her.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Please, Gorko am, wadu Munyal,"</span> she pleaded with her husband.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Mi wadata!"</span> He yelled back at her and stormed out of the house, while Fantah was crying, and her mother was trying to comfort her.</p>
        
        <p>Everywhere she went, people made fun of her as if she was some joke. Even little girls who used to fear her now bumped into her and sang in jest when she passed them at the stream or market.</p>
        
        <p>However, what hurt her most was how much she missed her stranger. Despite everything, she still could not bring herself to hate him. She loved him so much that she was willing to accept him back into her life the very moment he returns if he ever comes back.</p>
        
        <p>She prayed and prayed, crying herself to sleep every night. The weight of her loneliness and shame brought her to the brink of despair, and she even started having suicidal thoughts.</p>
        
        <p>As she was returning home with the cows the next evening, her father was seated in his usual dinner place in front of the house with his brother Ballo. She knelt and greeted them. Her uncle answered her greetings, but her father didn't. So, she got up and went inside. When she brought their food later, she overheard them discussing a marriage between her and Sa'adu in a few days. It was to be a low-key marriage with no celebrations whatsoever, and he would not bring any dowry. Her world was shattered into pieces. The last shred of dignity she had was finally gone. She ran back to her mother, crying.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Wadu munyal bingel am, stop crying,"</span> her mother cooed.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"How could he do that to me, Dada? It's not fair."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I know, but Fantah, considering everything that happened and how the villagers are humiliating us, I think this is the best option that we have. Don't you think?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"No Dada, I don't want to marry Sa'adu. I don't love him."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Oh hush, girl! What do you know about love? You have not lived long enough to know how cruel life can be, believe me. Love is when you find someone to care for you no matter what. And it's better to be with someone who loves you than someone you love."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"What? That doesn't make any sense."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Well then, I am sure this will. You are going to marry your cousin on Friday, whether you like it or not!"</span></p>
        
        <div class="scene-divider">…………………………………..</div>
        
        <p>As soon as they had had breakfast the next day, Fantah ran to Diddi's house. It was a small Ruga located at a corner of the village up on a hill. Unlike most young boys who got married early and continue to live in their parents' house with their wives, Murtala, Diddi's husband, worked hard and built his own Ruga far away from his parents. He wanted to be alone with his wife, away from meddling relatives. He was a very proud man, and he worked his fingers to the bone to justify it.</p>
        
        <p>As she approached the Ruga, she was impressed by how beautiful it looked. There were two large domes; probably one was for Diddi and the other for Murtala. There was a smaller one for grains, and just like their father's compound, the cows had their own place at the back of the compound. It had a beautifully designed raffia fence of different colors, and the entrance had a gate made from woven cane. She peeked inside and noticed how clean the compound was. It was swept so clean that you could see the stylish marks that the broom left behind. It was deliberate. That was Diddi's signature style of sweeping. There were some wild flowers growing in neatly arranged shapes, and she noticed that they had been recently watered. An open kitchen was by the side, and if her guess was right, the toilet and bathroom should be behind the hut.</p>
        
        <p>The house was located on top of a hill, which allowed anyone in a standing position to have a scenic view of the planes. It was breathtaking! It was the beginning of the rainy season, and the sight of green grasses and trees looked like someone had placed them in their positions. The sky was pregnant with clouds - although she had a feeling it was not going to rain immediately - arranged in deep purple and gray colors, making the whole view picture-perfect.</p>
        
        <p>Standing there and taking all this in, she felt a deep pang of jealousy. Not because she wasn't happy for her sister. No, that wasn't it. It's just that this was what she had wanted for herself all her life, exactly like that. Not a big house, or servants, or guards, or royal treatment of any kind. This was what she wanted. A cute little Ruga on a hill to share with the man she loves and to raise their children, peacefully. That was all.</p>
        
        <p>She was far gone in her thoughts; she didn't notice Diddi coming out from behind her hut.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Fantah!"</span> She squealed in her sweet innocent young voice, then ran to open the woven cane gate and hugged her. Fantah could tell that she was pregnant even though it wasn't obvious yet; she had gained some weight. Her skin was lighter, and she had a beautiful glow on her face. And this time, she truly felt a tinge of jealousy for her sister.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Diddi!"</span> Fantah hugged her too, and without any warning, tears started flowing freely from her eyes.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Fantah, are you alright? Is everything at home alright?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I just missed you."</span> She lied, and they both cried.</p>
        
        <p>Then Diddi led her to her hut where Fantah opened up and told her everything that had been happening to her.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I know,"</span> Diddi said. <span class="dialogue">"I have heard a lot. I have had cause to constantly defend myself everywhere I go because of it, even from my in-laws."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Oh, Diddi, I am so sorry."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"No, it is alright, Fantah. You are my sister, and I love you. I can't stand seeing you suffer like this. I wish there was a way I can help."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Baba wants to marry me off to Sa'adu next tomorrow."</span> She burst out crying again.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Well, don't you think it's better than staying single and waiting for your stranger? He may never come…"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"He will. I am sure of it!"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Alright, alright."</span> Diddi tried to calm her down. At that moment, she heard a gentle salutation at the gate, and her husband walked in. She heard him open the big water pot under the guava tree and pour some water inside. They were low on water supply, and he had gone to the stream to fetch water. Unlike other men who insist on only their wives fetching water for the house from the stream, Murtala preferred to go himself. He loved his wife and thought she was too delicate to carry a heavy pot of water on her head, especially in her current condition.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Aha! Addirawo, mi homnima."</span> He smiled shyly and greeted Fantah as he came into the hut.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Gorko amin mi homnima."</span> She gave out a little laugh and knelt to greet him.</p>
        
        <p>He was about to leave again when Diddi asked him to stay and went ahead to tell him about the situation on the ground.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"So, what are you going to do now?"</span> He asked Fantah.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I don't know. Even if I am going to marry Sa'adu, I need to hear from my stranger to be sure he doesn't want me anymore, because I am not sure that is the case. Maybe something happened to him. Maybe his wife is using black magic on him. Who knows? I just need to know."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Then go."</span> Murtala said.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"What?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Go and find out before the marriage."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"But the marriage is in two days' time."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Then, leave tonight. What do you think, Diddi?"</span> He asked his wife.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I agree. You need to leave tonight, Fantah. If they don't see you, the marriage will not take place."</span></p>
        
        <p>She was quiet for a moment. <span class="dialogue">"I think you are right. I will have to leave immediately. I know my way to Balda and where to find him."</span> She was suddenly very nervous.</p>
        
        <p>Murtala got up and reached into the topmost pot on a stack of pots that were arranged carefully in the room. They were given to Diddi as a wedding gift by her mother. He brought the pot down and opened it. There were some coins in it, and he counted ten and gave them to Fantah.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"What…? No, I… can't…"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Please, take it and go to find your beloved. Believe me, I understand perfectly well what it's like to be in love, and I don't know what I would do if I was denied being with the love of my life."</span> Fantah burst into tears again.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Come on, stop that, Addirawo. You need to go home and get ready. Leave when your parents are asleep. I will wait for you by the stream and escort you to the forest, ok?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Ok. Thank you."</span></p>
        
        <p>He turned to his wife. <span class="dialogue">"I must go back to work now. The water you asked for is outside."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Thank you, my husband. Allah hokku sa'a."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue foreign-language">"Amin."</span> He said and left.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"So, Fantah, you know what to do now. Make sure to sneak out through the back entrance as soon as Dada goes to sleep, ok?"</span> Diddi warned her.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Oh, my sister."</span> She hugged her and continued crying.</p>
        
        <p><strong>[End of Chapter Eighteen]</strong></p>

      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 19: A Leap of Faith!</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                                       
      <p>That night, as Fantah lay awake, waiting for her father to retire to his hut, her mind was filled with all kinds of thoughts about how difficult it would be to leave the house without being heard. It would be easy to sneak out of her mother's room because her mother slept deeply, and even if she was caught, she could lie and say she was going to the bathroom. But her father was always alert at night. He might hear her sneaking out and mistake her for an intruder, and that wouldn't be funny. She might end up as the recipient of his poisonous arrows! With the way things were between them now, she was sure he wouldn't even mourn her. Then, there was also the issue of wild animals in the forest. Would she make it to Balda alive? She remembered her terrible experience with the lion and how Hayah, like a knight in shining armor, shot the beast and saved her life, and her heart was stirred with love for him all over again. Well, this time she would be alone to cross the forest by herself in the dead of the night.</p>

      <p>She thought about Hayah and what she might find out. Why hasn't he come? Has he changed his mind? Did he decide to stay and work things out with his wife? Will he even welcome her if she gets there? Most importantly, is he alive and well? Another wave of fear engulfed her.</p>

      <p>At that moment, she heard her father closing the main entrance. He then went to their hut to check up on them and bid them good night. Her mother was already fast asleep but opened her eyes and groggily said good night to her husband, then went back to sleep. A few moments later, she heard the door of his hut close. She waited for a while to make sure he had gone to sleep, then got up and picked up her little bundle that she had hidden behind the bed. With her shoes in her hand, she quietly opened the door. At that moment, her mother moved. She held her breath and froze where she was standing, but to her relief, her mother did not wake up. So, she slid out and closed the door quietly behind her. There was no sound coming from her father's hut, indicating that he was fast asleep.</p>

      <p>"Thank God," she whispered as she tiptoed toward the bathroom area, and then to the wall with the large opening that drains out water during the rainy season, to prevent the house from being flooded. She threw the bundle over the wall, then bent down and crawled through the drainage opening. It was dry because it hadn't rained for days; her slender body easily went through it. "Thank God," she whispered again. As soon as she was successfully outside the house, she picked up her little bundle and ran fast to the stream where Murtala was waiting for her.</p>

      <p>"Are you sure you still want to do this?" he asked her.</p>
      <p>"I want to… I need to," she replied.</p>

      <p>He escorted her deep into the forest, and when it was beginning to get bright, he bid her farewell and headed back home. There was no turning back now. She turned and faced east and kept on walking towards the city of Balda.</p>

      <p>Hayah was in his wife's room playing with his young son who was about two and a half years old. His happy little voice filled the room with laughter as his father swirled him around. Talatu was already pregnant with another child. He was happy, happier than he had been in a long time. Granted, he was having some political problems, but on the domestic front, he was as happy as can be.</p>

      <p>He also didn't seem to think of Fantah anymore. In fact, he seemed to have forgotten about her. It was almost as if she never existed, and that was what Talatu had always wanted. Maybe it was the child and the pregnancy that did it. Maybe it was the charms she had been sprinkling on their bed before they slept and the one her uncle prepared from the scoop of sand she got from his footprints, or maybe it was the long silence between him and Fantah because all his messages were being intercepted. But whatever it was, it worked in her favor. He was paying more attention to her now and looked happy doing it. Life couldn't be sweeter if you poured honey on it.</p>

      <p>At that moment, a servant walked into the room to inform him that Fantah had arrived. He immediately put down his son and walked, almost running, out of the room to where she was. Talatu's little happy world suddenly came crashing down before her.</p>

      <p>At the door, Fantah looked tired and dirty. She appeared to have been crying, but as soon as their eyes met, she knelt to greet him. He went to her and placed his hands on her shoulders, then lifted her up to him.</p>

      <p>"I have missed you, my love," he said, almost in tears himself, and she bent her head down shyly, tears running freely from her eyes. He led her to the garden, away from prying ears.</p>

      <p>"I missed you so much."</p>
      <p>"I miss you too, my stranger."</p>

      <p>"I am sorry I couldn't come all those years; I have been under severe pressure from the new Lamdo. But I did send a lot of messages to you which you never replied to. I feared you must have married someone else."</p>

      <p>"I never got any of your messages."</p>

      <p>"What? I have sent over fifty messages. I thought you were angry with me and married someone else." She went ahead to tell him all that had transpired back at home.</p>

      <p>"And no, I am not married, yet," she said.</p>

      <p>"Fantah..."</p>

      <p>"Stranger, I came here because I want to be with you. I couldn't bear the thought of living without you. My father plans to marry me off to someone, but I ran away."</p>

      <p>"But your father will never give us his blessing."</p>

      <p>"Then let me be your concubine."</p>

      <p>"What?"</p>

      <p>"Yes, my stranger. My father will never give us his blessing. I can't be with you without being married, and I refuse to marry someone else. So, that is the only solution."</p>

      <p>"You are a freeborn, Fantah; you know what that means."</p>

      <p>"I know. But I would rather lose my freedom to you than to anybody else." She knelt. "Allah rene."</p>

  <div id="page-20" class="page-container">
    <div class="hero" style="background-image: url('https://cdn.pixabay.com/photo/2022/11/02/06/42/toadstool-7564167_1280.jpg')">
      <div class="hero-content">
        <h1>Chapter 20: The Foe!</h1>
        <p class="subtitle"></p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="content">
      <div class="chapter-content">
                                         <p>"How did this happen? More importantly, how could you let this happen?" The Waziri fumed, pacing back and forth in Talatu's room. It had been one week since Fantah came back to Balda, and he was only getting to see Talatu that day because he had been avoiding Hayah like a plague. He knew Fantah was in town because he had eyes and ears everywhere. As soon as he learned of her arrival, he sent for Auwalu, the messenger who had pretended to be delivering all of Hayah's letters to Fantah in the past three years but, in reality, delivered them into the hands of the Waziri, and dispatched him out of town before Hayah could find and interrogate him. Then he waited until Hayah had left town before going to see his niece.</p>

  <p>"I did not let it happen, uncle. She just came back on her own," Talatu protested.</p>

  <p>He paused and thought for a few seconds. "The efficacy of the potion must have waned. I need to get you another one."</p>

  <p>"I don't want another one."</p>

  <p>"Excuse me?"</p>

  <p>"I don't want to use charms anymore... it did not work, and it is not right, and it doesn't even work." Her heart was beating so fast, she thought she was going to faint. She had never defied him before. That was the first time she stood up to him, and it caught him by surprise. He was speechless for a while.</p>

  <p>"Ok. Have it your way," he said and left.</p>

  <p>But there was still the matter of Auwalu, the messenger, to take care of. If Hayah ever finds him, he will pay with his life. Even though the Waziri had sent the messenger away, far from Balda, it didn't stop Hayah from sending search parties to find him, and they brought him back. He was lashed harshly with leather whips until he confessed. Hayah was furious. He wanted to kill the Waziri, but he knew he couldn't do that. In fact, he didn't have the power to even kill this particular messenger, with the current Lamdo as the Waziri's puppet. So, he instructed his servants to lock him up.</p>

  <p>However, he found a way to vent his anger on Talatu by ignoring her completely. He stopped going to her bed and spent all his nights in Fantah's room. He stopped going to her room to speak to her or even to eat any food she prepared. When she gave birth to their second son, an event that should have brought him great joy, he still ignored her. He only organized the naming ceremony but barely acknowledged the boy, who was named Mu'azu after his uncle.</p>

  <p>He bestowed all his affection on Fantah. He gave her gifts of clothes, jewelry, horses, lands, and even slaves, including things she didn't even need to make her happy. He also took her on many outings around Balda and beyond. He wanted to show her the world, starting with his domain. His followers and subjects admired her and showered her with gifts. Even when he was preaching in the mosque, she would be in the female section listening. Since most people had never laid eyes on the princess, they mistook Fantah for her.</p>

  <p>Fantah was delighted with all of it, taking everything in with the innocence of a child. She appreciated the gifts, and it was always a delight for her to meet his friends and subjects. She quickly learned how to interact well with them, which made them love her even more. She also used her knowledge of nature to help him make decisions concerning his domain. A case in point was when the people of Balda experienced a drought, and the river almost dried up, with the food supply getting low.</p>

  <p>She advised him to dredge the river and channel the water from the highland towards the town. It was a system the Bororo always adopt to survive in dry and harsh terrains. Granted, it was mostly done on a smaller scale, but in this instance, Hayah had both the manpower and the resources to do it on a larger scale. Within a short time, the river was flowing into Balda, and it gave its inhabitants a new lease on life. To many of them, it was a miracle and proof that the man deserved veneration as a deliverer. His followers increased in droves, so fast that it set off alarm bells in the surrounding towns.</p>

  <p>It gave Hayah much pleasure. Fantah wasn't just a pretty face, but one with an intelligent mind. He imagined her as queen beside him when he finally regains the throne as the Sultan of Sokoto. Such were the kind of beautiful thoughts he was having lately. Silently, he prayed for her to give him a son, an heir as gracious as herself. Unbeknownst to him, the answer to his prayer was already on the way.</p>

  <p>One fateful day, he took her on a tour of his maize farm at the outskirts of Balda, escorted by one of his trusted lieutenants, Adamu, and ten servants. They were having a good laugh as he showed her around the farm and the soon-to-be-harvested ears of corn. Suddenly and without warning, she fell off her horse! She was rushed back home, and a traditional healer was brought in to attend to her while a Mallam recited prayers outside.</p>

  <p>But Hayah, in spite of several assurances to be calm from well-wishers, kept pacing nervously from end to end in front of her apartment. After a couple of moments that seemed like forever, he was given the good tidings; she was expecting a child!</p>

  <p>"Alhamdulillah!" he exclaimed, and everyone present chorused their congratulations. She would make him a father soon. He couldn't have wished for something better, and his love for her grew even stronger.</p>

  <hr>

  <p>Talatu sat in her room, lost in thoughts. She had heard the rumor that Fantah was pregnant. It was something she had feared from the beginning but knew it was inevitable. "What if she gives birth to a son?" she thought. "That would be the end of me." Even though she already had two sons, she knew that Fantah's son would still be favored ahead of hers in Hayah's eyes, and she was not about to let that happen.</p>

  <p>When her father told her that she was going to marry Hayah, she couldn't have been happier. He was the fantasy of many Balda's maidens back then - handsome, young, and brave. A prince from the most prominent Fulani royal family. To marry such a man was a dream come true. Even though he wasn't a direct heir to the throne, there was a chance of him ascending it if his uncle and his two sons were to die before him. Effectively, he was fourth in line. Being a princess herself, she knew the system; she had heard enough to know that it wasn't entirely impossible. In times of war and unrest, things had a way of changing in an instant. Moreover, even if he did not become the Sultan, there was a slight possibility he could usurp power in Fombina. That was what her uncle had feared all along. At first, she did not like the idea, but now that her father, the Lamdo of Maroua, was dead and the title was no longer with their immediate family, she welcomed the idea wholeheartedly. Either way, she would be queen, and her sons would get to inherit great power.</p>

  <p>However, that dream was being threatened by this unfortunate turn of events. This young Bororo girl came out of nowhere and was threatening to take everything away from her. Even if she was just a concubine, it wasn't unheard of for the son of a king's concubine to inherit the throne. It had happened more than once before. When a king names his successor before his eventual demise, the kingmakers will be left with no choice. She wouldn't let that happen. Her uncle had been right all along. This girl was nothing but trouble.</p>

  <p>In the beginning, she never cared about it because she was in love with Hayah. She had admired him, even before he noticed her. When they got married, she built so many palaces in the air about a happily-ever-after. But he never loved her. He treated her worse than a concubine, all because of this whore, Fantah. All the love she had for him now turned into pure hate, and she wanted him to suffer, to pay for every tear that she had shed. Both of them would have to pay. If getting in bed with the devil is what she had to do, then she would do it, for her children and for revenge!</p>

  <p>"Are you sure about this?" The Waziri asked her. She had called on him earlier to apologize and ask for his help.</p>

  <p>"Yes, Kawu. I am ready to do anything."</p>

  <p>"I thought you loved him?"</p>

  <p>"I used to. But love is nothing but fool's gold. What I want now is power."</p>

  <p>"You know, you will have to do some things that you may not be comfortable with, things that may be too extreme," he cautioned.</p>

  <p>"I am ready to sell my soul to the devil, if that is what it takes."</p>

  <p>Her determination frightened him. "That's enough!"</p>

  <hr>

  <p>Their plan required time and patience. For it to work, Talatu needed to swallow her pride and endear herself to Fantah. First, she went and apologized to her and pretended to be intimidated by her, while also insinuating that she was just a jealous rival who had learned her lesson and would never do it again.</p>

  <p>Naively, Fantah believed her and let her guard down. For three months, they were the best of friends. Every day Talatu would visit her, and at other times Fantah would return the favor, and they would talk for hours, sharing personal stories and beauty secrets. Talatu even went ahead to tell her what she thought Hayah preferred in bed. And they giggled.</p>

  <p>Fantah cherished their new friendship and even felt sorry for judging Talatu harshly at the beginning. She tried to imagine herself in Talatu's shoes, then concluded that if she were in the same situation, she might have done what Talatu did to save her marriage too. Touched by this thought, she forgave Talatu wholeheartedly.</p>

  <p>Talatu manipulated Fantah to plead with Hayah on her behalf. That night, as Fantah was serving Hayah his supper, she waited until he was very relaxed and then brought up the issue of Talatu. He tensed up immediately.</p>

  <p>“Did she hurt you?”</p>

  <p>“No, my stranger,” she replied softly. “In fact, she has been so nice to me. She seemed genuinely sorry for what she did and wants to make peace.”</p>

  <p>“Really?”</p>

  <p>She could see that he did not believe her. “Yes, my stranger. I feel like I understand why she did what she did. It is because she loves you so much. I would have done the same thing if I thought I was going to lose you.” She sat next to him and gave him a pleading look. “Please, my beloved, give her another chance. She will not do it again.”</p>

  <p>Hayah gave her a quizzical look and felt sorry for her. She was so naive in matters of power and probably incapable of understanding the ruthlessness of royal families; cold-blooded snakes who would do anything to hang on to power. And so, for the sake of peace and the love he had for Fantah, he decided to accept her plea and forgave Talatu.</p>

  <p>“All right my beloved, I have forgiven her. But be careful, things are not always what they seem around here.”</p>

  <p>“Thank you, my stranger,” she said happily.</p>

  <p>Sadly, she fell for an elaborate ruse from Talatu. While they were playing besties, Talatu was stealing some personal items from her, items that her uncle would use as ingredients in a very volatile and wicked spell to get rid of her once and for all.</p>

  <p>At first, she stole her comb, but soon after helping her to braid her hair. She wanted the residue of hair in it for her charms. Next, she cut a little piece from her wrapper before it was washed. After that, she got some of her nail clippings, and finally, she gathered a handful of sand from the footprints that Fantah left at the front of her apartment.</p>

  <p>All the items were then handed over to her uncle, who took them to the Boka. The Boka cast a spell on the items, then tied them up in a black piece of cloth and gave it back to him with instructions on what to do.</p>

  <p>One week later, the news came from Yola of the death of Lamido Sanda, with a successor appointed immediately. The coronation of the new Lamido was planned for the following Friday, and other minor Lamdos and notable men like Hayah were expected to attend, which meant he would be away for at least a week, leaving Fantah alone and vulnerable. It was an opportunity Talatu and her uncle, the Waziri, were waiting for.</p>

  <p>The day after Hayah left for Yola, the Waziri got a black pigeon and tied a little black bundle to its foot. He then went on top of a hill and waited until just before midnight and started to chant.</p>

  <p>First, he held the bird in his hands and faced the East, saying, "Oh, jinn of the West, hear my plea and send my enemy on an errand. Here is my gift. Please accept."</p>

  <p>Then he turned and faced the West, saying, "Oh, jinn of the East, hear my plea and send my enemy on an errand. Here is my gift. Please accept."</p>

  <p>Next was the South, as he said, "Oh, jinn of the South, hear my plea and send my enemy on an errand. Here is my gift. Please accept."</p>

  <p>Finally, he faced the North, saying, "Oh, jinn of the North, hear my plea and send my enemy on an errand. Here is my gift. Please accept."</p>

  <p>Then he returned to his initial position and released the bird. It flew towards the North, then turned East.</p>

  <hr>

  <p>That night, Fantah did not pray before going to bed. She always prayed the Isha Prayer before going to sleep, but for some reason, she felt too tired to do that, probably due to the physical exertions of the pregnancy. She intended to do so but decided to catch a few winks first.</p>

  <p>While she slept, deep in her subconscious, she heard someone calling her name. It was almost as if she was dreaming or in a trance, but it felt so real. Somehow, she got up and walked towards the voice. Quietly sidestepping her servants Ladi and Asabe who were sleeping at the foot of her bed, she reached the door. Her headgear fell to the ground, but she didn't stop. She evaded the guards at the main entrance and stepped out. Almost on impulse, she looked up and saw a black pigeon flying toward the North, and she followed it. Barefooted, without a veil or scarf over her head, she kept following it until she left the town behind her.</p>

  <p><em>[End of Chapter Twenty]</em></p>

        <p class="subtitle"></p>

  <p>At the first call to prayer in the morning, Fantah's female servants woke up to find her bed empty. At first, they thought she had gone to the toilet. So, Asabe decided to quickly go to the servant's bathroom to relieve herself before their mistress returned. However, at the door, she noticed the silk headgear on the floor and instinctively knew something was wrong. She quickly alerted Ladi, and they went outside to inquire from the guards, who claimed they hadn't seen anyone leaving.</p>

  <p>From there, they rushed to inform Talatu of the situation but were told that she was still sleeping. They had to wait for a nerve-wracking four more hours before she finally "woke up."</p>

  <p>"Are you sure she didn't just wander off? You know those nomads; they hardly stay in one place for long," Talatu said derisively, showing her lack of concern.</p>

  <p>"Yes, Hajiya, I'm sure," Asabe answered nervously, still crying. "She would not just leave like that. She would have woken us up first."</p>

  <p>"Well, let's wait and see if she shows up. If she doesn't by afternoon, I will send out a search party," Talatu said callously.</p>

  <p>Asabe and Ladi looked at each other with horror in their eyes. Asabe tried to suggest sending the search party immediately, but Talatu dismissed the idea and ordered them to wait for Fantah.</p>

  <p>Around two in the afternoon, Hayah returned home, tired and hungry. He was eager to eat and rest before going to see Fantah. But as he entered his house, he noticed Asabe's tearful expression.</p>

  <p>"What is it, Asabe?" he asked, fearing the worst.</p>

  <p>"It's my Madam…" she stammered.</p>

  <p>"What about her?" Hayah asked anxiously.</p>

  <p>"We woke up this morning, and she was gone."</p>

  <p>"Gone? Where? Home?!" he questioned in distress.</p>

  <p>"I don't know. We found her headgear on the floor... and her shoes are still there…"</p>

  <p>"What are you saying, young woman!" He was almost shouting, and Asabe recoiled in fear.</p>

  <p>"She… I…" She couldn't speak.</p>

  <p>"Where have you searched for her?" Hayah summoned the guards and questioned them. They all claimed that they did not see her leave. Talatu heard the commotion and came out, pretending to be worried.</p>

  <p>"Welcome, Allah rene," she knelt, but he just stared at her and said nothing. "We were worried sick. I was about to send a search party to look for her." Asabe looked at Talatu in surprise. Talatu gave her an evil look, and the poor girl turned her face away.</p>

  <p>"You mean you haven't sent out a search party yet?"</p>

  <p>"I am sorry, Allah rene. I just found out..." He didn't wait for her to finish before mounting his horse and storming out of the house.</p>

  <p>Hayah quickly assembled a search party consisting of some of his best men and hunting dogs. They spread out in different directions to comb the town, surrounding villages, and forests. However, the first search efforts excluded Ndokula, as Hayah was sure she wouldn't be there due to their strained relationship. He had an idea of what might have happened, and his stomach tightened at the very thought of it. He prayed to God that his thoughts were wrong because that would be very sad indeed. Despite searching all day and all night, they could not find her.</p>

  <hr>

  <p>Somewhere in the forest, Fantah had just finished eating a mango fruit and threw away what was left of it. She had been surviving on fruits and drinking from the streams for the past three days. Her appearance was disheveled, and she emanated a strong odor. Her hair was wild and tangled from scratching, and her feet were covered in sores from walking barefoot. She kept following the cursed bird, driven by an inexplicable force pulling her towards it.</p>

  <p>The bird continued to fly, and she followed relentlessly, climbing up a steep mountain. At times, the bird would perch on a shrub, seemingly waiting for her to catch up, only to fly out of reach again just as she approached. Throughout her journey, she heard voices calling her name from a distance, adding to her confusion and distress.</p>

  <p>Fantah quickened her pace, driven by an intense determination to catch the bird. Reaching the top of the mountain, she looked down at the treacherous rocks below, making her feel dizzy. Despite the danger, she remained fixated on catching the elusive bird.</p>

  <p>Just as she was about to take a step that could lead to her horrible death, a familiar hand grabbed her. It was Hayah, her savior. He swirled her around to face him, and her eyes, bloodshot from crying and terror, met his. With a weak smile, she tried to speak, but her throat was sore. In that moment of relief and reunion, she blacked out, finally succumbing to exhaustion and despair.</p>

  <hr>

  <p>She woke up on her comfortable bed back in Balda, surrounded by several people reciting the Qur'an and praying for her. Silently, she thanked God before blacking out again.</p>

  <p>After finding her, Hayah felt immense gratitude towards God for keeping her safe. He knew how fortunate he was to have located her in the nick of time after three long days of searching. Taking her home, he instructed her female servants to give her a warm bath and tend to her wounds. He then summoned forty prominent Mallams from the realm to come and pray, seeking to exorcise the evil spirit that had possessed her. Each Mallam recited the Qur'an from beginning to end in an effort to break the spell of the Jinn. Additionally, a traditional healer came with oils to treat her wounds and provided her with some herbs.</p>

  <p>However, amidst the relief of finding Fantah, there was also tragic news for Hayah – Fantah had suffered a miscarriage. Overwhelmed with grief and consumed with hatred, a feeling he had never experienced towards anyone before, he directed his anger towards Talatu at that moment. He said a silent prayer, seeking strength, as he feared what he might do to her in his fury.</p>

        <h1>Chapter 22: Allies and Enemies</h1>
        <p class="subtitle">"Trust is the rarest jewel in the desert" - Tuareg proverb</p>

          <p>Fantah was restless, pacing nervously back and forth in her room. It had been two weeks since her return, and Hayah had placed extra guards at her apartment to ensure her safety. She wasn't allowed to leave the apartment; he feared she might disappear again.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Salamu Alaikum,"</span> she heard him say as he entered her room.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Alaikum Salam, my stranger,"</span> she knelt to greet him.</p>
        
        <p>He walked over to her and lifted her to her feet. They sat next to each other on the bed. <span class="dialogue">"How are you feeling today?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Like a caged animal,"</span> she sulked.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"That means you are much better now,"</span> he laughed.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I am sorry. I am not used to staying in a room all day, not to talk of for two weeks!"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Well, you just have to get used to it. At least until you are alright. Also, you are not a little girl anymore, so you have to start living your new status and age."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I am sorry, my stranger."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"It is alright. I am only doing this because of my love for you. And I am sorry for what she did to you. I will make sure she pays for it."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Thank you, my stranger. But I have been thinking... I know what she did to me was wrong, but I have also been hurting her."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Oh,"</span> he said in surprise. <span class="dialogue">"How so?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"She is a woman like me, with feelings too. She is the first wife, the lady of the house, and yet, you spend more time with me and even pay more attention to me. That is bound to make any woman jealous."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Would you rather I spend all my time with her?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I am just suggesting, my stranger, that you pay a little more attention to her, so she won't feel so bad. She is, after all, the mother of your sons. And if my stranger will be kind enough not to punish her, I will be eternally grateful."</span></p>
        
        <p>Hayah looked at her with a mixture of admiration and pity. He couldn't understand how a girl so young could be so well ahead of her age. <span class="dialogue">"Alright, I will think about it,"</span> he said.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Perhaps you can start by spending the night in her apartment tonight."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Are you chasing me away?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"No, my stranger, I will never dream of doing that,"</span> she said, smiling. He burst out laughing and playfully tweaked her nose.</p>
        
        <p>Nevertheless, Hayah continued to shun his wife for what she did to Fantah while also keeping Fantah under close surveillance for her own safety. She was never allowed to leave her room under any circumstances. Everything she needed was provided for her with a guard always placed in front of her apartment and another at the back. He could never forgive Talatu for what she did. <span class="highlight">"Poor Fantah,"</span> he thought, so innocent and trusting. She never suspected Talatu's evil intentions, and it almost killed her. He thought about how very close to dying Fantah was when he caught her hand. If he had gotten there a second later, she would have been dead by now.</p>
        
        <p><span class="highlight">"How dare you, Talatu!"</span> he thundered. He was angry at the Waziri too. He needs to find a way to deal with him permanently, even though he was the new Lamdo's right-hand man. There must be a way, and he was going to find it.</p>
        
        <p>But at that moment, he had a bigger problem to take care of. He began to suspect that the new Lamido of Yola, Lamido Zubbairu, did not like him. The whole time he was in Fombina, he had been paying homage to Hammoa and Iya from the other royal families of Yola, hoping that one of them would be the next king. But as fate would have it, Zubbairu from a rival royal family was appointed instead, much like what happened in Maroua. Furthermore, his spies had informed him that the Lamdo of Maroua and the Waziri had been visiting the new monarch and having discussions about him, which was a matter of interest. He also knew that Lamido Zubbairu had been in contact with the Sultan of Sokoto, who also considered him a threat. Time was running out on him, and it was time to do something to secure his position. Previous attempts to ally himself with the Muharib, the king of Sudan, had come up short, as the Muharib's influence and authority were both limited and did not extend to Sokoto nor Yola. He needed a new powerful ally with both military power and resources to actualize his ambitions.</p>
        
        <p>Presently, the Lamdo of Maroua had been able to limit his access to military equipment and soldiers. While he had many followers, they were not soldiers and would be useless in the battlefield. He needed able-bodied and trained men who would be loyal to him. A storm was coming, and he needed to prepare himself.</p>
        
        <p>To make matters even worse, he heard that his cousin Umaru, the Sultan of Sokoto, had formed an alliance with the white infidels. He had heard of their superior weaponry and how no one had been able to defeat them in their quest to take over lands from Africans. It was as if they came out of nowhere and started conquering kingdom after kingdom, slowly from the East, then North. And now they had their claws firmly embedded into the seat of the Caliphate. He suspected that his cousin, the reigning Sultan, might want to leverage their support to come after him. It was time to act, and fast.</p>
        
        <p>Earlier, he had heard incredible tales of a ruthless Sudanese rebel known as Rabbeh, who had been conquering sizable portions of lands for himself. The man was fearless and loved to fight dirty. Such a man interested him. If only he could form an alliance with him to fight his cousin and regain the throne!</p>
        
        <p>He confided in Fantah about his intentions and left in disguise to Rabbeh's camp. The camp itself was at Baghirmi in the outskirts of the Kanem-Borno Empire. But the man's whereabouts were shrouded in secrecy. What was in plain view, though, was his grand agenda to usurp the Mai's throne, the pre-eminent king of the Kanuri of the Kanem-Borno Empire, second only to the Sokoto Caliphate in power and prominence.</p>
        
        <p>On his secret orders, Hayah's spies had made contact with one of Rabbeh's most trusted commanders, who arranged a meeting. At the meeting, he planned to solicit the rebel's assistance and, in return, offer him military secrets he had acquired over the years, as well as resources if he succeeded in becoming the Sultan of Sokoto according to plan.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Are you sure about this, my stranger?"</span> Fantah asked him when he told her about his intentions.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Yes, my love. Rabbeh has the resources I need to help me in my quest. I need him."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"But he is a rebel. He is not known to be fair in his endeavors and may not be a man of his word."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Don't worry about that. I have everything planned. I am going to make him an offer he can't refuse, and I only need him for a short time,"</span> he reassured her.</p>
        
        <p>She still wasn't convinced. <span class="dialogue">"Alright, but what happens when your absence is noticed? Won't that get you in trouble?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I have made arrangements for that. Before we got together, it was widely known that I take time off for hunting, and I disappear for weeks at a time. I have told Arabu to quietly spread the word that I am on one of such trips. No one will suspect anything. In the meantime, I have taken measures to ensure your safety. I will station several guards to protect you night and day, and Arabu will oversee them and check on you in case you need anything else. Under no circumstance should you leave your apartment, do you understand me?"</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Yes, my stranger."</span> But she still wasn't happy. <span class="dialogue">"God be with you."</span></p>

        <p>Hayah left the next day at the break of dawn, accompanied by his trusted lieutenant, Adamu, and two guards. They rode for five days, traversing the sparse savannah grassland of Adamawa, enduring the harsh desert heat until they reached Rabbeh's military camp at Baghirmi. The camp was situated close to an oasis in the middle of the desert, somewhere inside Kanem-Borno, along the Chadian border. As they arrived, they noticed some of Rabbeh's men, recognizable by their dusty brown pantaloons and jumpers, along with black turbans wrapped over their heads and faces to protect against the harsh desert climate and to maintain their fearsome appearance.</p>
        
        <p><span class="highlight">"These men are not soldiers,"</span> Hayah thought. They were criminals, engaging in theft and terrorizing innocent travelers along the lucrative Trans-Saharan trading routes. Desperation drove him to consider working with such unsavory characters.</p>
        
        <p>At the camp's entrance, they were stopped and searched, but Hayah quickly presented his royal seal, expediting their passage through security checks. They walked through a forested area, witnessing beautiful trees and a large lake. The lush surroundings contrasted with the violent activities of the camp's inhabitants. Hayah couldn't help but feel sympathy for some captives he spotted, knowing that he would try to help them once he had the opportunity.</p>
        
        <p>Eventually, they reached a clearing in the center of the camp, where several tents were arranged in a circular formation around a central fire. Women were cooking, and Hayah couldn't help but feel that these hooligans had despoiled the natural beauty of the place.</p>
        
        <p>The guards led him to one of the ordinary-looking tents, but the interior was surprisingly lavish and well-decorated. The tent's opulence convinced Hayah that this must be Rabbeh's tent. Luxurious Persian rugs adorned the floor, accompanied by cushions and fluffy pillows. The bed, draped in beautiful sheets and surrounded by curtains, offered privacy and comfort. Fragrances filled the air, and artifacts and flowers decorated the tent.</p>
        
        <p>A study table with books and a chair completed the ensemble, creating an atmosphere of luxury and class.</p>
        
        <p>He sat down to wait with his trusted lieutenant, Adamu, and one of the soldiers. A few moments later, Rabbeh walked in. He was dressed in similar clothes to his men and had a large sword dangling from its sheath by his right side. When he entered, he removed his turban, and Hayah was surprised by his handsome features – tall and slender, probably due to a poor diet or constant exertions in the desert. The man was light-skinned and had soft, black, curly hair of the Shua Arabs. When he smiled, his perfect set of teeth was a delight to behold. And then, he embraced Hayah.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Salaamu Alaikum, your highness. My people informed me of your arrival. Welcome. I hope your journey was safe,"</span> he said while guiding him to one of the cushions and sitting on another one beside him.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Thank you, my brother. But you can call me by my name,"</span> Hayah replied.</p>
        
        <p>They exchanged more pleasantries, and shortly, refreshments and food were served. Hayah wanted to get down to business immediately, but Rabbeh insisted he needed to rest first, and then they would talk in the morning. Not wanting to offend him by refusing his hospitality, Hayah obliged.</p>
        
        <p>He was given a private tent, almost as impressive as Rabbeh's, but he insisted on his men being there with him. He did not trust Rabbeh and his band of rebels. He opted for safety in numbers as a wise choice. His soldiers took turns, staying up to watch over each other, just in case. But nothing happened.</p>
        
        <p>In the morning, they were all invited to Rabbeh's tent, where they had a feast of breakfast. They also got to meet Rabbeh's son, Abdou. A splitting image of his father, he looked like he was in his late teens, but Rabbeh told Hayah that the boy was twenty-one and already a commander in his army. Hayah was both impressed and surprised. At first glance, Rabbeh himself seemed too young to have a son like that, but after a closer look, he realized that it was his exceptionally good looks and his slender body that made him appear so young. He was, in fact, a few years older than Hayah.</p>
        
        <p>After breakfast, Hayah's men went back to their tent while he stayed behind to have a one-to-one discussion with Rabbeh. Abdou also excused himself to give them some privacy.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Why should I help you?"</span> Rabbeh asked after Hayah had told him of his plans and made him an offer to train his people. <span class="dialogue">"You have nothing of value that I want."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I have good military knowledge and an ironclad ambition, just like you. I am sure we can help each other."</span></p>
        
        <p>Rabbeh smiled. <span class="dialogue">"My soldiers are well-trained, highly motivated, and have proven themselves in battles so far. I don't see where you are going with this."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Yes, your soldiers are good. But for you to embark on your next expedition of conquering the Kanem-Borno Empire, you need more than just fearless soldiers. You need cunning, tactics, and strategies, all of which I am good at and ready to impart that knowledge on your men. In return, I will want you to help me in my own quest."</span></p>
        
        <p>Rabbeh thought for a while. <span class="dialogue">"Your expertise is legendary. I could use an ally like you. But I want more than just your expertise. There is one more thing you will have to do for me if you want my help."</span></p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"I will do anything,"</span> Hayah said.</p>
        
        <p><span class="dialogue">"Alright then. This looks like the start of a beautiful friendship,"</span> Rabbeh went ahead and told him what he wanted from him.</p>

// Like functionality
function toggleLike(pageId) {
  const likeKey = `page_${pageId}_likes`;
  let likes = JSON.parse(localStorage.getItem(likeKey)) || { count: 0, liked: false };

  likes.liked = !likes.liked;
  likes.count = likes.liked ? likes.count + 1 : Math.max(0, likes.count - 1);

  localStorage.setItem(likeKey, JSON.stringify(likes));
  updateLikeUI(pageId, likes);
}

function loadAllLikes() {
  for (let i = 1; i <= 14; i++) {
    const likeKey = `page_${i}_likes`;
    const likes = JSON.parse(localStorage.getItem(likeKey)) || { count: 0, liked: false };
    updateLikeUI(i, likes);
  }
}

function updateLikeUI(pageId, likes) {
  const likeBtn = document.getElementById(`likeBtn-${pageId}`);
  const likeCount = document.getElementById(`likeCount-${pageId}`);

  if (likeCount) likeCount.textContent = likes.count;
  if (likeBtn) {
    if (likes.liked) {
      likeBtn.classList.add('liked');
      likeBtn.innerHTML = `❤ Liked (<span id="likeCount-${pageId}">${likes.count}</span>)`;
    } else {
      likeBtn.classList.remove('liked');
      likeBtn.innerHTML = `❤ Like (<span id="likeCount-${pageId}">${likes.count}</span>)`;
    }
  }
}

// Comment functionality
function addComment(pageId) {
  const commentInput = document.getElementById(`commentInput-${pageId}`);
  const comment = commentInput.value.trim();
  if (!comment) return;

  const commentKey = `page_${pageId}_comments`;
  let comments = JSON.parse(localStorage.getItem(commentKey)) || [];

  let username = localStorage.getItem('comment_username');
  if (!username) {
    const name = prompt('Please enter a name to display with your comment:');
    if (!name) return;
    username = name;
    localStorage.setItem('comment_username', username);
  }

  const newComment = {
    id: Date.now(),
    text: comment,
    date: new Date().toISOString(),
    author: username
  };

  comments.unshift(newComment);
  localStorage.setItem(commentKey, JSON.stringify(comments));
  renderComment(newComment, pageId);
  commentInput.value = "";
}

function loadAllComments() {
  for (let i = 1; i <= 14; i++) {
    const commentKey = `page_${i}_comments`;
    const comments = JSON.parse(localStorage.getItem(commentKey)) || [];
    comments.forEach(comment => renderComment(comment, i));
  }
}

function renderComment(comment, pageId) {
  const commentsContainer = document.getElementById(`comments-${pageId}`);
  if (!commentsContainer) return;

  const commentDiv = document.createElement('div');
  commentDiv.className = 'comment';

  const author = document.createElement('div');
  author.className = 'comment-author';
  author.textContent = comment.author || 'Anonymous';

  const date = document.createElement('div');
  date.className = 'comment-date';
  date.textContent = new Date(comment.date).toLocaleString();

  const text = document.createElement('p');
  text.textContent = comment.text;

  commentDiv.appendChild(author);
  commentDiv.appendChild(date);
  commentDiv.appendChild(text);
  commentsContainer.appendChild(commentDiv);
}
